# Resource Library

The definitive reading/watching/listening list for serious screenwriters. Ranked by impact. Don't try to consume all of this at once — pick one book, read it cover to cover, then write. Repeat.

---

## Books — Read These In Order

### Tier 1: The Foundation (Read Before You Write a Word)

**Save the Cat! — Blake Snyder**
The commercial script bible. If you want to write something that sells, this is the first book. Snyder's 15-beat sheet is the industry's most used structural framework. Read it, internalize it, then learn when to break it.
> Follow up with: *Save the Cat Strikes Back* and *Save the Cat Goes to the Movies* (applies the method to 50 films — invaluable)

**Story — Robert McKee**
The deep craft bible. McKee goes underneath structure into the nature of story itself — how scenes work, how value shifts drive narrative, how subtext operates. Dense and demanding. Worth every page.
> This is the long game. Read Snyder first, McKee second.

**The Anatomy of Story — John Truby**
Truby's system is different from Snyder's or McKee's — it's character-first, premise-first. His concept of the "moral argument" and 22-step structure is uniquely powerful for writers who want thematic depth. Some of the best character work in any craft book.

---

### Tier 2: The Craft Layer

**The Writer's Journey — Christopher Vogler**
Joseph Campbell's Hero's Journey translated specifically for screenwriters and adapted from Vogler's famous 7-page studio memo that circulated Hollywood in the 80s. Essential for understanding mythic story structure. Works alongside the beat sheets, not instead of them.

**Adventures in the Screen Trade — William Goldman**
Goldman wrote Butch Cassidy, All the President's Men, The Princess Bride. This book is part memoir, part industry insider guide, part practical craft manual. His chapter on structure and his demolition of how Hollywood actually works is required reading. Goldman's line "Nobody knows anything" is the most honest thing ever written about the film industry.

**Which Lie Did I Tell? — William Goldman**
The follow-up to Adventures. Just as good. More craft, more inside stories, a complete screenplay dissected scene by scene.

**Making a Good Script Great — Linda Seger**
Where Snyder gives you the map, Seger gives you the diagnostic tools. Her rewrite-focused approach helps you identify exactly what's wrong with a draft and why. Best book on the revision process.

---

### Tier 3: The Deep Dives

**The Art of Dramatic Writing — Lajos Egri**
Written in 1946. Still the most precise analysis of premise, character, and conflict ever written. Egri's concept of the "bone structure" of a character predates every modern framework. Dry but foundational.

**Screenplay: The Foundations of Screenwriting — Syd Field**
The original mainstream craft book — Field essentially invented the three-act framework for screenwriting in the 1970s. Read it to understand where everything else came from. His later books (The Screenwriter's Problem Solver, Selling a Screenplay) are underrated.

**The Coffee Break Screenwriter — Pilar Alessandra**
Practical, accessible, and excellent for getting unstuck. Her approach works in short sessions — good for writers who don't have 8-hour blocks. Her podcast (On the Page) is equally good.

**Writing Drama — Yves Lavandier**
European perspective on structure that challenges some American orthodoxies. His treatment of dramatic irony and the three-act form is the most precise analysis of *why* those structures work at a psychological level.

---

### Industry / Business

**The Hollywood Standard — Christopher Riley**
The definitive guide to correct screenplay format. Every formatting question answered. If BetterFountain generates something that looks wrong, Riley's book has the answer.

**Selling Your Story in 60 Seconds — Michael Hauge**
The pitch bible. Hauge's six-stage structure and his approach to the verbal pitch is industry-tested. If you're going to pitch in a room, know this book.

**Writing Movies for Fun and Profit — Thomas Lennon & Robert Ben Garant**
The most honest book about the assignment market ever written. Lennon and Garant have made millions writing commercial movies (Night at the Museum, Herbie). Their advice is blunt, funny, and contrarian. Required reading for anyone who wants to work as a professional screenwriter rather than just write a great spec.

## Produced Scripts — Read At Least 20 Before You Write

Reading produced scripts is the most efficient craft development tool that exists. You learn more from 10 great scripts than from 5 craft books.

### Where to Find Them
- **simplyscripts.com** — massive free library, organized by genre
- **imsdb.com** (Internet Movie Script Database) — another large free library
- **screenplays.io** — clean, readable format
- **The Black List** (blcklst.com) — hosts new unproduced specs; also sells produced scripts
- **The WGA Library** — wga.org — online access to historical scripts

### Scripts Every Serious Writer Should Read

**Structure / Commercial Craft:**
- *Chinatown* (Robert Towne) — the perfect screenplay. Study every scene.
- *Die Hard* (Jeb Stuart, Steven de Souza) — the template for contained action
- *The Silence of the Lambs* (Ted Tally) — character and thriller mechanics working together
- *Tootsie* (Larry Gelbart, Murray Schisgal) — the B Story and theme done perfectly
- *Groundhog Day* (Danny Rubin, Harold Ramis) — premise as character arc

**Dialogue:**
- *Glengarry Glen Ross* (David Mamet) — the master class in subtext and pressure
- *When Harry Met Sally* (Nora Ephron) — voice and structure in perfect balance
- *The Big Lebowski* (Coen Brothers) — voice as world-building
- *Good Will Hunting* (Damon/Affleck) — character-driven dialogue that carries weight

**Elevated Genre / Modern:**
- *Get Out* (Jordan Peele) — thematic precision through genre mechanics
- *Parasite* (Bong Joon-ho) — structure and tonal shifts at the highest level
- *Hereditary* (Ari Aster) — how to build dread through character
- *Moonlight* (Barry Jenkins) — what "economy of storytelling" actually means

**Prestige Drama:**
- *Spotlight* (Tom McCarthy, Josh Singer) — procedural as moral argument
- *No Country for Old Men* (Coen Brothers) — adapt this for how to handle theme without statement
- *There Will Be Blood* (Paul Thomas Anderson) — character as thesis
- *12 Years a Slave* (John Ridley) — adaptation and structure under thematic weight

## Screenwriting Software

**Final Draft** (finaldraft.com) — $100
Industry standard. Every professional uses it. Production departments expect it. If you're serious, get it. The collaboration features and revision tracking are built for professional production.

**Highland 2** (Mac only) — $50
Beautiful, Fountain-based, thoughtfully designed. Best experience for solo writing on Mac. Excellent for writers who want to stay in plain text.

**Arc Studio Pro** (arcstudiopro.com) — Free tier / $99/yr
Modern interface, strong outlining tools, cloud sync. Best browser-based option. The free tier is genuinely usable.

**WriterDuet** (writerduet.com) — Free tier / $99/yr
Best for co-writing. Real-time collaboration built in. If you have a writing partner, start here.

**VS Code + BetterFountain** — Free
What this repo uses. Write in plain text Fountain, render in real time, export PDF. Zero cost, full control, version-controlled.

**Scrivener** — $59
Not screenplay-specific but excellent for managing large projects with research, character notes, and script simultaneously. Good if you're developing alongside this repo.

## Websites

**scriptshadow.net** — Carson Reeves reviews 3-5 scripts per week (spec and produced). His AF ("Impressive" / "Worth the read") ratings have industry weight. His "how to write a logline" and genre guides are as good as any book.

**deadline.com/tag/spec-sales** — Live feed of spec sales, option deals, and assignment news. Read this weekly to understand what the market is actually buying.

**The Black List** (blcklst.com) — The most important platform for unproduced scripts. The annual Black List (top-rated unproduced scripts as voted by development executives) has launched hundreds of careers. Host your script here. Get it evaluated. An 8+ score surfaces to managers.

**Go Into the Story** (gointothestory.blcklst.com) — The Black List's official blog. Scott Myers posts daily — script analysis, Q&As with working writers, career advice. Free, deep, essential.

**trackingboard.com** — Tracks development deals, spec sales, and projects in development. Free digest version available. Their "Young & Hungry" list of hot spec scripts circulates among Hollywood executives.

**IMDbPro** (pro.imdb.com) — ~$150/yr
The industry's contact and project database. Find production companies, locate managers and agents, see what's in development. Essential once you're actively submitting.

**The WGA Website** (wga.org) — Register scripts ($20), access the schedule of minimums (what they're required to pay you), find a signatory list, research credits.

## Podcasts

**Scriptnotes** (John August & Craig Mazin)
The best screenwriting podcast that exists. August wrote *Charlie's Angels*, *Go*, *Big Fish*, the *Charlie and the Chocolate Factory*. Mazin wrote *Chernobyl* and the *Hangover* trilogy. They cover craft, industry, contracts, career — everything. Back catalog is enormous. Start with any episode that matches your current question.
> scriptnotes.net or wherever you get podcasts

**Script Apart** (Al Horner)
Deep-dive interviews with working screenwriters about the early drafts of their films — the draft before the polish, before the producers got involved. Guests have included the writers of *Parasite*, *1917*, *Marriage Story*, *Moonlight*. Craft-focused and excellent.

**On the Page** (Pilar Alessandra)
The accessible, practical craft podcast. Alessandra teaches through specific script problems. Good for writers who want diagnostics — what's wrong with my script and why.

**The Screenwriting Life** (Meg LeFauve & Annie Moellering)
Two working Hollywood writers talking about the craft and business honestly. Meg wrote *Inside Out* and *Captain Marvel*. Underrated, grounded, and real.

---

## YouTube

**Tyler Mowery** (@TylerMowery)
The best screenwriting YouTube channel. Deep dives on story theory — his breakdowns of theme, character arc, and structure are better than most books. Start with his videos on "want vs. need," "the misbelief," and his film analyses. Watch his breakdown of *Parasite* and *Whiplash*.

**Just Write** (@justwrite)
Excellent analyses of what works and what doesn't in specific films and scripts. Less prescriptive than Mowery — more observational, which is valuable.

**Lessons from the Screenplay** (@LessonsFromTheScreenplay)
Side-by-side analysis of book vs. screenplay adaptations. Illuminating for understanding what works on the page vs. the screen.

**StudioBinder** (@StudioBinder)
Production-focused but has strong screenplay content — breakdowns of scene types, shot types, and how production decisions trace back to script choices.

**MasterClass — Aaron Sorkin Teaches Screenwriting**
$15/mo. Worth it just for Sorkin's lessons on intention vs. obstacle and how he thinks about scene construction. His episode on writing without a net (no outline) is fascinating even if you don't write his way.

**ScreenCraft Academy** — online courses tied to their competition ecosystem

**Austin Film Festival Online** — workshops and panels archived online; worth the membership

**The Black List Education** — script coverage, workshops, pitch opportunities

## The One Rule

Read 20 produced scripts. Pick one structure book. Write a script. Then read the next 20 scripts and write the next script. Nothing replaces pages. The books are the map. The scripts are the territory.
