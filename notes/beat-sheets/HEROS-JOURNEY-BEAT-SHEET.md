# The Hero's Journey Beat Sheet

Joseph Campbell's monomyth, adapted for screenwriting by Christopher Vogler in *The Writer's Journey*. This is the deep structure underneath nearly every myth, fairy tale, and epic story ever told. It's not a formula — it's a map of how human transformation actually works.

Use this for stories about profound personal transformation, mythic/epic narratives, and adventure. It's not a substitute for Three-Act or Save the Cat — it works alongside them.

---

## THE 12 STAGES

---

### Stage 1: The Ordinary World
**What it is:** We see the hero in their normal environment — before the adventure. This establishes what they have to lose and what's missing from their life.

**The craft:** The Ordinary World should feel *incomplete*. Something is off. The hero may not know it, but we sense it. This is the wound before it's named.

**Famous example:**
*The Lion King* — Simba's ordinary world is Pride Rock. He's playful, beloved, heir to the kingdom. But Scar is already circling. Something is wrong in the shadows.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Hero's ordinary world:
What's missing or incomplete in their life:
The wound they haven't faced yet:
What they believe about themselves/the world:
```

### Stage 2: The Call to Adventure
**What it is:** Something disrupts the ordinary world. A problem, a challenge, an invitation. The hero is presented with an opportunity to step into a larger world.

**The craft:** The call should feel both compelling and threatening. It must matter. If there's no real reason to answer, there are no real stakes.

**Famous example:**
*The Wizard of Oz* — The tornado. Dorothy is swept away from Kansas. The ordinary world is literally destroyed.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```

Who or what issues it:
Why it matters to the hero specifically:
What opportunity or threat does it represent:
```

---

### Stage 3: Refusal of the Call
**What it is:** The hero hesitates or outright refuses. Fear, doubt, obligation, or comfort holds them back.

**The craft:** This is normal and human. If the hero accepts immediately without hesitation, we don't believe the stakes. The refusal makes the eventual acceptance meaningful.

**Famous example:**
*Star Wars* — Luke says he can't go with Obi-Wan. He has responsibilities on the farm. He's afraid. He refuses twice before circumstances force his hand.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
How the hero refuses or hesitates:
What's holding them back (fear, obligation, comfort):
What makes the refusal believable:
```

---

### Stage 4: Meeting the Mentor
**What it is:** A wise figure enters the hero's life and provides tools, wisdom, or encouragement needed for the journey. The mentor may not physically accompany the hero into the special world.

**The craft:** The mentor gives the hero what they need — but never everything. If the mentor could solve all problems, there'd be no story. They equip but don't rescue.

**Famous example:**
*The Matrix* — Morpheus. He shows Neo the truth, gives him training, and believes in him. But Neo has to face the Agents alone.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Who is the mentor:
What they provide (tools, wisdom, belief):
What they deliberately withhold or cannot give:
How the mentor relationship ends or transforms:
```

---

### Stage 5: Crossing the First Threshold
**What it is:** The hero commits to the adventure and enters the Special World. This is the point of no return. The Ordinary World is left behind.

**The craft:** This must feel decisive. The hero makes an active choice. The door closes behind them. This is equivalent to "Break into Act Two" in the three-act structure.

**Famous example:**
*The Lord of the Rings* — Frodo leaves the Shire. He crosses the boundary at the edge of the known world. He can't go back to being just a hobbit.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The threshold they cross:
Why it's a point of no return:
What they leave behind:
What Special World they enter:
```

---

### Stage 6: Tests, Allies, and Enemies
**What it is:** The hero navigates the Special World. They meet new friends and enemies, learn the rules of this new reality, and face early challenges that test their resolve.

**The craft:** This is the Fun and Games section of Save the Cat. The audience is learning the world alongside the hero. The hero is also learning who they can trust.

**Famous example:**
*Raiders of the Lost Ark* — Indy navigates the marketplace, deals with Belloq, reconnects with Marion, deals with the Nazis. Each encounter tests what he's made of.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Key tests the hero faces:
Allies encountered (and what they represent):
Enemies encountered (and what they oppose):
Rules of the Special World the hero must learn:
```

---

### Stage 7: Approach to the Inmost Cave
**What it is:** The hero and their companions prepare for the major ordeal. They approach the dangerous place where the central crisis will occur. There may be setbacks, regrouping, planning.

**The craft:** Tension builds. This is the quiet before the storm. Character relationships are tested. The hero may doubt themselves here.

**Famous example:**
*Star Wars* — The approach to the Death Star. The preparations, the briefing, the moment before the trench run. Everyone knows this is the big moment.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What the "inmost cave" is (the place/situation of greatest danger):
How the hero prepares:
What doubts or tensions arise here:
The last thing that happens before the ordeal:
```

---

### Stage 8: The Ordeal
**What it is:** The hero faces their greatest crisis. They must confront their deepest fear, fight their most dangerous enemy, or face death — literal or metaphorical. This is the "all is lost" moment. Something dies so something new can be born.

**The craft:** This is the death-and-rebirth beat. The hero must die in some way — give up who they were — to become who they need to be. The resurrection on the other side is not guaranteed.

**Famous example:**
*Finding Nemo* — Marlin and Dory are swallowed by a whale. Marlin must let go — literally let go of the whale's tongue and trust the fall into darkness — to be spit out alive. He must surrender control to survive.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The ordeal (the crisis):
What must die (what the hero loses or must surrender):
How the hero faces death (literal or metaphorical):
What's reborn on the other side:
```

---

### Stage 9: The Reward (Seizing the Sword)
**What it is:** The hero survives the ordeal and claims the reward — a treasure, knowledge, a power, a relationship. But the real reward is the internal change: the hero now knows who they are.

**The craft:** The reward should feel earned. It's not a random gift — it's the direct result of surviving the ordeal. The hero has changed.

**Famous example:**
*Star Wars* — Luke destroys the Death Star. The rescue is complete. The rebels celebrate. But the real reward is Luke's transformation from farm boy to hero.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The external reward (treasure, knowledge, victory):
The internal reward (who the hero has become):
How it was earned through the ordeal:
```

---

### Stage 10: The Road Back
**What it is:** The hero begins the journey back to the Ordinary World, but often faces one more challenge. The forces of the old world push back. The adventure isn't over.

**The craft:** This complication raises the final stakes. The hero has the reward but isn't home yet. Something threatens to take it away or undo what they've accomplished.

**Famous example:**
*The Wizard of Oz* — Dorothy gets the broomstick but the Wizard tries to renege on his promise. She's not back in Kansas yet. The journey back has obstacles.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The complication on the road back:
What threatens the reward or the return:
Why the hero can't just coast home:
```

---

### Stage 11: The Resurrection
**What it is:** The climactic final test. The hero faces one last crisis — a final exam that tests whether they've truly been transformed. This is the film's climax. The hero must prove their change is real and permanent.

**The craft:** The resurrection is different from the Ordeal. In the Ordeal, the hero transformed. In the Resurrection, they demonstrate that transformation. The old self must die completely so the new self can live.

**Famous example:**
*The Silence of the Lambs* — Clarice enters Buffalo Bill's house alone, in the dark, terrified. She faces her fear completely — not with backup, not with safety. She earns her badge.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The final crisis:
How it tests whether the hero has truly changed:
How the hero's transformation provides the solution:
The moment of resurrection (when the new self fully arrives):
```

---

### Stage 12: Return with the Elixir
**What it is:** The hero returns to the Ordinary World, transformed, carrying something of value — a lesson, a treasure, a change — that can benefit others. The world is better for the hero having made the journey.

**The craft:** The elixir doesn't have to be literal. It can be wisdom, love, freedom, truth, or justice. What matters is that the hero brings something back. A story about pure personal transformation without impact is incomplete.

**Famous example:**
*The Lion King* — Simba returns to Pride Rock. He defeats Scar. He takes his place as king. The elixir is his rightful identity — reclaimed — and the restoration of the Pride Lands. He brings life back to a dying world.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What the hero returns with (literal or metaphorical):
How the Ordinary World is better because of this:
How the hero is different from who they were at Stage 1:
The final image of the transformed hero:
```

## Hero's Journey vs. Save the Cat

They're not competing frameworks — they're describing the same structure from different angles.

| Hero's Journey | Save the Cat Equivalent |
|----------------|------------------------|
| Ordinary World | Opening Image + Setup |
| Call to Adventure | Catalyst |
| Refusal of the Call | Debate |
| Crossing the First Threshold | Break into Two |
| Tests, Allies, Enemies | Fun and Games |
| Ordeal | All Is Lost |
| Road Back + Resurrection | Break into Three + Finale |
| Return with the Elixir | Final Image |

Use Save the Cat for pacing and commercial structure. Use the Hero's Journey for the deeper thematic and psychological architecture. The best scripts satisfy both.
