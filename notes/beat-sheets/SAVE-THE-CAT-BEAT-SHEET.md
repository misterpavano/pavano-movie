# Save the Cat Beat Sheet

Blake Snyder's 15-beat system. The most widely used structural framework in commercial Hollywood screenwriting. If you want to write something that sells, know this cold.

The title comes from a technique: in your Setup, have your hero do something likable before they do anything heroic. "Save the cat." Make us care before you ask us to care.

---

## THE 15 BEATS

---

### Beat 1: Opening Image
**Page:** 1
**What it is:** A single image, a snapshot of the hero's world before the story begins. It's the "before" picture. By the end of the film, this image will have transformed.

**The craft:** It should be visual, immediate, and emotionally resonant. It sets tone, genre, and the protagonist's internal state — without being on the nose.

**Famous example:**
*The Silence of the Lambs* — Clarice Starling runs alone through the woods. She's disciplined, isolated, striving. A "TRAINEE" sign. She's not there yet.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Image:
What it tells us about the hero:
What it tells us about the theme:
```

---

### Beat 2: Theme Stated
**Page:** 5
**What it is:** Someone — not the hero — says something that captures the film's central message or question. The hero doesn't understand it yet. The audience might not either. By the end, everyone will.

**The craft:** It's rarely a speech. It's usually oblique. A throwaway line that isn't throwaway.

**Famous example:**
*Legally Blonde* — Warner tells Elle: "If I'm going to be a senator by the time I'm 30, I need to stop dressing like a... like a..." She fills in: "Like what?" Implicit theme: people underestimate her because of how she looks. She'll spend the whole film proving them wrong.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Who says it:
The line:
What it actually means (subtext):
```

### Beat 3: Setup
**Pages:** 1-10
**What it is:** We meet the hero in their world. We see the six things that need to change — the flaws, the relationships, the wounds, the stasis that is making the hero's life unsatisfying even if they don't know it yet.

**The craft:** Plant everything that will pay off in Act Three. This section is all setup and no payoff — but the audience doesn't know that yet.

**Famous example:**
*Bridesmaids* — Annie is broke, in a terrible situationship, her best friend just got engaged, and she has no direction. Every problem is introduced. Every relationship is established.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Hero's world:
The six things that need to change:
1.
2.
3.
4.
5.
6.
What's planted for later payoff:
```

### Beat 4: Catalyst
**Page:** 12
**What it is:** The inciting incident. The event that kicks the story into motion. Something happens to the hero — from outside — that changes everything.

**The craft:** It must be specific, irreversible, and story-engine-starting. Life before the catalyst cannot continue as it was.

**Famous example:**
*The Hangover* — Doug is missing the morning after the bachelor party. The story officially begins.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The event:
Who/what causes it:
Why it's irreversible:
```

---

### Beat 5: Debate
**Pages:** 12-25
**What it is:** The hero hesitates. Should they answer the call? This is where we make the stakes real — the status quo must be genuinely appealing for the debate to have weight.

**The craft:** The hero must have a real reason to resist. If there's no reason to stay, there are no stakes for leaving.

**Famous example:**
*The Matrix* — Neo keeps waffling. He goes back to his cube job. He tries to ignore the white rabbit. He keeps telling himself this can't be real.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What the hero debates:
Why staying is genuinely tempting:
What finally tips them toward action:
```

---

### Beat 6: Break into Two
**Page:** 25
**What it is:** The hero makes an active choice that commits them to the story. They leave the old world behind. Act Two begins.

**The craft:** This must be the hero's active decision — not something done to them. They choose to enter the upside-down world.

**Famous example:**
*Tootsie* — Michael Dorsey, dressed as Dorothy, walks into the audition. He's committed. No going back.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The decision:
Why it can't be undone:
What "new world" they're entering:
```

---

### Beat 7: B Story
**Page:** 30
**What it is:** A new character or relationship enters the story. This subplot is where the theme lives and breathes. It's usually a love story, a mentor, or a profound friendship.

**The craft:** The B Story character often says the thing the hero needs to hear. They embody the theme. By Act Three, the A and B Stories must collide.

**Famous example:**
*Shrek* — Donkey joins Shrek. The B Story is literally about accepting friendship from someone you're trying to push away. That's the whole theme.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
B Story character:
Their relationship to the hero:
How they embody the theme:
```

---

### Beat 8: Fun and Games
**Pages:** 30-55
**What it is:** The promise of the premise. What the audience paid to see. The hero is in the new world, and we're watching the premise in action. This is pure genre delivery.

**The craft:** This section should feel fun, exciting, or emotionally engaging in whatever mode your genre demands. It's not plot-heavy — it's experiential.

**Famous example:**
*Back to the Future* — Marty is in 1955. He finds his parents. He tries not to screw up the timeline. Each scene is a delight. The premise delivers.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What the audience came to see (premise promise):
Key scenes that deliver this:
How the hero is growing (or refusing to grow) here:
```

---

### Beat 9: Midpoint
**Page:** 55
**What it is:** A major event at the exact middle of the script. Either a false victory (things look great — disaster is coming) or a false defeat (things look terrible — the hero will rise). Stakes are raised. The hero's goal may shift.

**The craft:** False victories must be followed by complications that make them worse. False defeats must deepen the stakes toward a third-act confrontation. Either way: everything changes at the midpoint.

**Famous example (False Victory):**
*Toy Story* — Buzz and Woody escape Sid's garage... only to end up trapped in his yard, missing the truck. Celebration → disaster.

**Famous example (False Defeat):**
*10 Things I Hate About You* — Kat finds out Patrick was paid to date her. Everything she'd built emotionally is destroyed. She's lower than when she started.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
False victory or false defeat:
What changes or is revealed:
How stakes escalate from this point:
```

### Beat 10: Bad Guys Close In
**Pages:** 55-75
**What it is:** The external antagonist is gaining power and the hero's internal flaw is reasserting itself. The team is fracturing. Things are getting worse from every direction.

**The craft:** This is not one event — it's a sustained pressure campaign. The hero is being squeezed from outside AND inside simultaneously.

**Famous example:**
*Jurassic Park* — The raptors have escaped. The systems are down. The group is separated. Grant is alone with the kids and no backup coming. Every page is worse than the last.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
External antagonist gaining strength (how):
Internal flaw reasserting itself (how):
Team fractures or relationships strained:
The escalating pressure:
```

### Beat 11: All Is Lost
**Page:** 75
**What it is:** The lowest point. The worst moment. The thing the hero has been working toward is gone, impossible, or destroyed. There must be a "whiff of death" — something or someone important dies, or feels like it dies.

**The craft:** It must feel unrecoverable. If the audience thinks "oh they'll fix that easily," it doesn't land. Death — literal or metaphorical — is the key.

**Famous example:**
*The Avengers* — Coulson is killed by Loki. The team has failed. They're not even a team. The helicarrier is damaged. The Tesseract is gone. Everything is broken.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What is lost or destroyed:
The whiff of death (what dies):
Why it feels unrecoverable:
```

---

### Beat 12: Dark Night of the Soul
**Pages:** 75-85
**What it is:** The hero sits with the loss. They reflect, despair, and hit rock bottom emotionally. They must find — from within — the reason to get back up.

**The craft:** The answer to "how do I get back up" must come from the theme. It's not external rescue — it's internal transformation. This is where the hero finally earns their arc.

**Famous example:**
*Rocky* — Rocky tells Adrian he can't win. He just wants to go the distance to prove he wasn't a bum. It's honest. It's defeated. And it's exactly the right answer — because not needing to win is what lets him fight without fear.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
How the hero processes the loss:
The internal question they sit with:
What the theme suggests the answer is:
The moment they decide to try again:
```

---

### Beat 13: Break into Three
**Page:** 85
**What it is:** The hero has the answer. A new plan, a new insight, a synthesis of A Story and B Story. They're ready to storm the castle.

**The craft:** The solution must come from the hero's internal growth. It should incorporate what the B Story character taught them. The hero at page 85 is fundamentally different from the hero at page 1.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The new insight or solution:
How it reflects the hero's arc:
How A and B Story synthesize here:
```

---

### Beat 14: Finale
**Pages:** 85-110
**What it is:** The final battle. In five movements:
1. Gathering the team
2. Executing the plan
3. High tower surprise (plan hits a complication)
4. Dig deep down (hero reaches into who they've become)
5. The execution of the new plan — victory

**The craft:** The finale must resolve both the external conflict (defeat the antagonist) AND the internal conflict (complete the arc). One without the other is unsatisfying.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The final confrontation:
The high tower surprise (unexpected complication):
How the hero's arc provides the solution:
The resolution of both A and B Stories:
```

---

### Beat 15: Final Image
**Page:** 110
**What it is:** The mirror of Beat 1. The same or similar setup — completely different meaning. The visual proof of transformation.

**The craft:** Less is more. A look. A gesture. A callback line that now means something different. The audience should feel it without needing it explained.

**Famous example:**
*The Silence of the Lambs* — Clarice is now Agent Starling at her graduation. She receives a call from Lecter. She stands straighter. She's not a trainee anymore. The woods she ran through alone are behind her.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Final image:
How it mirrors the opening image:
What's different (the transformation):
The last thing we see:
```
