# Three-Act Structure Beat Sheet

Fill this in for your script before you write page one. Every cell should be specific and concrete — character names, locations, specific events. Vague beats produce vague scripts.

A feature screenplay is approximately 90-120 pages. One page = roughly one minute of screen time.

---

## ACT ONE — Setup (Pages 1-30)
*Goal: Establish the world, the hero, and the problem. End with a point of no return.*

---

### Opening Image (Page 1)
**What it is:** A single visual that captures the "before" state of your story. It should contrast with your final image to show how much has changed.

**The rule:** It tells us genre, tone, and something essential about your protagonist — without a word of dialogue if possible.

> **Example (Die Hard):** John McClane on a plane, white-knuckling the armrests. A stranger tells him to take off his shoes and make fists with his toes. He looks ridiculous. He's out of his element.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Opening image:
Location:
What it tells us about the hero:
What it tells us about the world:
Contrast with final image (come back to this):
```

---

### Theme Stated (Page 5)
**What it is:** Someone — usually NOT the protagonist — says something that captures the film's central question or lesson. The hero doesn't understand it yet. They will by the end.

**The rule:** It's rarely on-the-nose. It's usually oblique. The audience files it away subconsciously.

> **Example (Titanic):** "A woman's heart is a deep ocean of secrets." Said by old Rose at the start. Jack and Rose don't understand this yet. We will.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Who says it:
What they say (actual line or close to it):
What it really means (thematic subtext):
Why the hero can't understand it yet:
```

---

### Setup (Pages 1-10)
**What it is:** We meet the protagonist in their ordinary world. Establish what they want, what they need (usually different), their flaw, and the stakes.

**The rule:** Plant everything you'll pay off later. Chekhov's gun. If it matters in Act Three, it's in the Setup.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Protagonist name:
Their world (job, relationships, environment):
What they WANT (external, conscious goal):
What they NEED (internal, often unconscious):
Their fatal flaw or wound:
Key relationships established:
Things planted for later payoff:
```

---

### Catalyst / Inciting Incident (Page 12)
**What it is:** The event that disrupts the protagonist's ordinary world and sets the story in motion. It happens TO them, not because of them.

**The rule:** It should be specific, visual, and irreversible. Life after the catalyst is different from life before it.

> **Example (The Matrix):** Neo is given the choice between the red pill and the blue pill.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What happens:
Why it's irreversible:
How the protagonist's world changes:
What question it raises for the audience:
```

---

### Debate (Pages 12-25)
**What it is:** The protagonist hesitates. Should they answer the call? This is normal — we'd all hesitate. It creates empathy and raises stakes.

**The rule:** The debate must feel real. The status quo must be genuinely tempting. Otherwise the stakes don't land.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What the protagonist is tempted to do (ignore the call):
What would they lose if they engage:
What's pushing them toward engagement:
The moment they finally decide:
```

---

### Break into Act Two (Page 25-30)
**What it is:** The protagonist makes a choice that commits them fully to the story's central action. No going back.

**The rule:** This is the protagonist's active choice — not something that happens to them. They step through a door they can't re-enter.

> **Example (The Silence of the Lambs):** Clarice agrees to interview Lecter. She walks into the basement.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The decision the protagonist makes:
Why it's a point of no return:
What new world they're entering:
What they're leaving behind:
```

---

## ACT TWO — Confrontation (Pages 30-90)
*Goal: Raise the stakes through escalating obstacles. The protagonist tries and fails (repeatedly) to get what they want. The pressure reveals who they really are.*

---

### B Story (Page 30)
**What it is:** A secondary storyline that usually carries the theme. Often a relationship — love interest, mentor, friend — that challenges the protagonist's worldview.

**The rule:** The B Story seems separate from the A Story but is actually about the same theme. They collide in Act Three.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
B Story relationship:
How it introduces/embodies the theme:
How it complicates the main plot:
How A and B intersect in Act Three:
```

---

### Fun and Games (Pages 30-55)
**What it is:** The "promise of the premise." What the audience came to see. The protagonist is in the new world, testing it out.

**The rule:** This section should deliver the core entertainment value of your genre. If it's a heist movie, we see the heist being planned. If it's a rom-com, it's the falling in love part.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What the audience came to see (the premise promise):
Key scenes that deliver on that promise:
How the protagonist grows (or fails to grow) during this section:
What obstacles they encounter:
```

---

### Midpoint (Pages 55-60)
**What it is:** A major reversal or revelation at the exact middle of the script. Either a false victory (things seem great — which means they're about to get worse) or a false defeat (things seem terrible — which raises the stakes toward Act Three).

**The rule:** Stakes must be raised at the midpoint. What seemed like the goal is now revealed to be insufficient or incorrect. The protagonist must raise their game.

> **Example (Star Wars):** Luke and the rebels escape the Death Star — only to realize they were tracked. The celebration is premature. Now the Death Star is coming for the Rebel Base.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
False victory or false defeat:
What's revealed or reversed:
How stakes escalate after this moment:
How the protagonist's goal shifts or clarifies:
```

---

### Bad Guys Close In (Pages 55-75)
**What it is:** The external forces of antagonism are getting stronger. The protagonist's internal flaws are also getting worse. Both are working against them simultaneously.

**The rule:** The protagonist's team — if they have one — starts to fracture. Trust breaks down. Things that were working stop working.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
External forces of antagonism gaining strength:
Internal flaws re-emerging or worsening:
Relationships under strain:
What starts to fall apart:
```

---

### All Is Lost (Pages 75-80)
**What it is:** The worst moment. The lowest point. Everything the protagonist has been working for is gone, dead, or impossible. Someone or something important may literally die (the "whiff of death" beat).

**The rule:** This must feel earned and devastating, not cheap. The audience should believe, even briefly, that the story cannot recover from this.

> **Example (Toy Story):** Buzz and Woody are trapped in Sid's house. Buzz discovers he's a toy. He's broken. Woody can't get through to him. They're going to miss the moving truck. All hope is gone.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
What is lost:
The "whiff of death" (what dies — relationship, hope, a character, an ideal):
Why it feels unrecoverable:
How the protagonist hits their absolute lowest point:
```

---

### Dark Night of the Soul (Pages 75-85)
**What it is:** The protagonist sits with the loss. They reflect. They're at rock bottom and must find a reason to get back up — not from outside, but from within.

**The rule:** This is the internal turn. The protagonist must finally confront the thing the B Story has been about. The answer to getting up is buried in the theme.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
How the protagonist processes the loss:
The internal question they must answer:
What the theme suggests the answer is:
What finally moves them to act:
```

---

### Break into Act Three (Page 85)
**What it is:** The protagonist finds a new solution, a new insight, or a new level of commitment. They have changed enough internally to try a different approach.

**The rule:** The solution must come from the protagonist's internal journey, not from outside help. It must feel earned.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
The insight or solution they find:
How it reflects their internal growth:
Why they couldn't have found this at the start:
What they now understand about the theme:
```

---

## ACT THREE — Resolution (Pages 85-120)
*Goal: Execute the climax. Resolve all open storylines. Show us who the protagonist has become.*

---

### Finale (Pages 85-110)
**What it is:** The final battle. Not just external (defeat the villain) but internal (defeat their own flaw). The protagonist must synthesize everything they've learned to win.

**The three-part Finale:**
1. **Gathering the team** — protagonist assembles resources, allies, and new understanding
2. **Storming the castle** — direct confrontation with antagonistic force
3. **High tower surprise** — the plan hits an unexpected complication at the worst moment
4. **Dig deep down** — protagonist reaches into the theme for the final answer
5. **The execution of the new plan** — victory through changed behavior

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Final confrontation setup:
The unexpected complication (high tower surprise):
How the protagonist's arc provides the solution:
How the climax resolves both A Story and B Story:
```

---

### Final Image (Page 110-120)
**What it is:** The mirror of the Opening Image. Same or similar setup — completely different meaning. Shows us the transformation.

**The rule:** You don't need to show it — the audience should FEEL it. Less is more. A look, a gesture, a single line. Not a speech.

> **Example (Die Hard):** McClane reunited with Holly. She introduces herself as Holly McClane — she's taken his name back. Everything that was broken is healed. No speech needed.

**YOUR SCRIPT:**
```
Final image:
How it mirrors the opening image:
What's different now (the transformation shown visually):
The last thing we see before black:
```
