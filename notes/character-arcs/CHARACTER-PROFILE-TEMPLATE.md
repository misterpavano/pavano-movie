# Character Profile Template

The difference between a character and a person is this: a person exists. A character exists *in service of a story*. Every choice about who they are should connect back to what the story needs.

Fill this out for every major character before you write their first scene. The decisions you make here will drive every scene they appear in.

## THE WANT — External Goal
*What does this character consciously want? What are they actively pursuing?*

This is the external, plot-level objective. It should be specific and achievable (or not). "To be happy" is not a Want. "To get the girl back before she leaves the country" is a Want.

```

## THE NEED — Internal Necessity
*What does this character actually need — the thing they don't know they need at the start?*

The Need is what the story is really about. The Want drives the plot. The Need drives the theme. At the end of the film, a character who gets their Want but ignores their Need is a tragedy. A character who surrenders their Want for their Need is a triumph.

```
The Need (what they truly need — often the opposite of what they want):
Why they can't see it yet:
What would have to happen for them to see it:
What recognizing the Need would cost them:
```

> **Classic Want vs. Need:**
> - *Casablanca*: Rick WANTS to be left alone (stay neutral, stay numb). Rick NEEDS to care again, to fight for something.
> - *Toy Story*: Woody WANTS to be Andy's favorite. Woody NEEDS to understand that love isn't a competition.
> - *Whiplash*: Andrew WANTS to be the best drummer. Andrew NEEDS to understand what being human costs him.

## THE GHOST — The Wound
*What happened in the past that shaped this character's worldview?*

Every character carries something from their past. The ghost is the formative wound — usually from before the story begins — that created their misbelief, their armor, their fatal flaw. You don't always need to show it. But you need to know it.

```
The wound (what happened):
When it happened (age, context):
Who caused it or was involved:
What the character decided about themselves or the world as a result:
```

---

## THE MISBELIEF — The Lie They Tell Themselves
*Based on the ghost, what does this character believe about themselves or the world that isn't true?*

The misbelief is the lie the wound created. It's the invisible glass wall between the character and the life they want. It feels like self-protection. It's actually self-sabotage.

```
The misbelief (the lie):
How it manifests in their behavior:
How it's preventing them from getting their Need:
What they'd have to accept to let go of it:
```

> **Examples:**
> - "I can't trust anyone" (wound: betrayal by parent)
> - "I don't deserve love" (wound: abandonment)
> - "The only way to survive is to be ruthless" (wound: powerlessness)
> - "If I let people in, I'll be hurt" (wound: loss)

## THE ARC — How They Change (Or Don't)

### Positive Arc
Character starts with a misbelief, struggles against it, and finally overcomes it. They get their Need even if it costs them their Want.

### Negative Arc
Character has an opportunity to change, refuses it, and ends worse than they began. The misbelief wins. Tragedy.

### Flat / Static Arc
Character's truth is tested by a corrupt world. They don't change — they change the world around them. (Think: action heroes, archetypal figures. Shane. Atticus Finch.)

```

```

### The Antagonist
**The antagonist is the mirror, not the villain.**

The best antagonists aren't evil for evil's sake. They represent the extreme version of what the protagonist could become if the misbelief wins — or they embody the argument the theme is arguing *against*. They make the protagonist's struggle real by being a credible alternative.

## THE SAVE THE CAT MOMENT
*What do we see them do in the first 10 pages that makes us like them before we're asked to root for them?*

This is the technique that gives Blake Snyder's book its name. Before your protagonist does anything heroic, have them do something human and likable. Not a speech. A moment. A behavior.

It can be:
- Helping someone who can't help them back
- Doing the right thing when it costs them
- A flash of vulnerability or humor
- A glimpse of the person they could be

```
