# Logline Template

Your logline is the single most important marketing document you'll write. Not the script. Not the treatment. The logline. If you can't nail a one-sentence version of your story, you don't understand your story well enough to write it.

Managers, producers, and executives hear thousands of pitches. Your logline has about 10 seconds. Make it count.

---

## The Formula

There are several versions of the logline formula. Learn all of them. Then forget the formula and make it feel effortless.

### The Core Formula
> **When [inciting event], [protagonist with defining trait] must [goal/action] before/or [stakes/deadline], but [antagonistic force/central conflict] stands in the way.**

### The Character-First Formula
> **[Protagonist descriptor] [wants/must] [goal] when [inciting event forces them to] [action] against [antagonistic force] in order to [ultimate stakes].**

### The Irony Formula
*Best for high-concept pitches where the idea itself is the hook.*
> **[Ironic premise in one sentence that raises an immediate question.]**
> Example: "A shark terrorizes a beach town and the one man who can stop it is afraid of water."

---

## The Four Ingredients Every Logline Needs

**1. A Compelling Protagonist**
Not just "a man" or "a woman." Give them a trait that matters to the story. "A burned-out cop," "a recently disgraced attorney," "a deaf teenager," "a con man who's finally gone straight."

**2. A Clear Antagonistic Force**
This can be a villain, a system, a situation, nature, or the protagonist's own psychology. It must be *specific*. "Evil forces" is not a logline. "A corporation that has erased all record of his existence" is a logline.

**3. Real Stakes**
What happens if they fail? It should feel consequential. Personal stakes are fine ("she'll lose her daughter") but must feel visceral. Stakes are better when they're both external AND internal.

**4. Irony or Unique Angle**
The best loglines have a "wait, that's interesting" quality. A twist that makes you want to know more. The cop who's afraid of the thing he has to fight. The assassin hired to kill his own family. The man who wakes up with no memory every morning but must solve his wife's murder.

---

## What Makes a Logline SELL vs. What Kills It

### Sells:
- Specific, vivid protagonist
- Clear, urgent stakes
- An ironic or unique angle that makes the premise feel fresh
- Implies a specific visual world
- Raises an immediate question in the reader's mind
- Strong active verbs

### Kills:
- "A young woman discovers..." (boring — discovery is passive)
- "...in a world where..." (lazy worldbuilding in a logline)
- Vague stakes ("everything is at stake")
- Two-sentence loglines (pick the more interesting one)
- Describing tone instead of story ("In this dark, comedic thriller...")
- Relying on a twist that doesn't land without context

## 20 Film Loglines Broken Down

**Jaws (1975)**
> A police chief with a fear of the ocean must protect a summer beach town from a massive great white shark — against a mayor who refuses to close the beaches.
- *Protagonist:* Fear of ocean = built-in irony
- *Stakes:* Public safety + livelihood of town
- *Antagonistic force:* The shark AND the mayor
- *Irony:* The man responsible for water safety can't face the water

**The Silence of the Lambs (1991)**
> An FBI trainee must seek the help of an imprisoned, brilliant cannibal to catch a serial killer who skins his victims — while the cannibal plays mind games designed to unravel her.
- *Protagonist:* Trainee (not yet qualified — stakes of proving herself)
- *Stakes:* The killer's next victim, and Clarice's sanity
- *Antagonistic forces:* Two villains, one overt and one covert
- *Irony:* She needs a monster to catch a monster

**Die Hard (1988)**
> A New York cop, visiting his estranged wife for Christmas, must single-handedly take down a group of terrorists who've seized her Los Angeles office tower.
- *Simple, clean, visual*
- *Irony:* Wrong time, wrong place, wrong city, wrong marriage situation
- *Stakes:* His wife, and everyone in the building

**Get Out (2017)**
> A Black man visits his white girlfriend's family estate and slowly uncovers a disturbing secret that puts his life in danger.
- *The logline holds back — deliberately*
- The horror comes from the gap between the mundane setup and the dark implication
- "Disturbing secret" works here because the audience fills in the horror themselves

**The Truman Show (1998)**
> A man slowly realizes that his entire life has been a live television show broadcast to the world — and must decide whether to escape the only reality he's ever known.
- *The second half (the decision) is what makes it a movie, not a premise*
- *Stakes:* Identity, freedom, truth
- *Irony:* He's famous. He doesn't know it. His entire life is a performance.

**Whiplash (2014)**
> A driven young jazz drummer at a prestigious music conservatory is pushed to the breaking point by a sadistic conductor who believes cruelty is the path to greatness.
- *Compact*
- *Conflict is the logline* — no villain, no twist, just two people in opposition
- *Stakes:* His talent, his humanity, his future

**Parasite (2019)**
> A poor Korean family cunningly infiltrates the household of a wealthy family as hired help — until a secret in the basement threatens to destroy everything they've built.
- *The "secret in the basement" is the turn that elevates it*
- *Stakes:* Their lives and their scheme
- *Irony:* The family preying on wealth has no idea what's already beneath them

**Knives Out (2019)**
> A renowned detective investigates the death of a wealthy crime novelist — but when the prime suspect becomes his unlikely ally, the case turns inside out.
- *"Turns inside out" = the irony baked into the premise*
- Works because it implies a genre subversion without giving away the trick

**Mad Max: Fury Road (2015)**
> A drifter allies with a rebel warrior woman fleeing a tyrant across a post-apocalyptic wasteland — in a relentless, non-stop chase.
- *Pure action premise — no subtlety needed*
- The logline IS the genre promise

**Hereditary (2018)**
> After the death of her secretive mother, a woman and her family begin to unravel disturbing secrets about their heritage — and something evil seems to follow them home.
- *Elevated horror logline* — the horror is implied, not stated
- "Disturbing secrets about heritage" + "something evil" = enough to create dread

**Groundhog Day (1993)**
> A cynical TV weatherman finds himself reliving the same day over and over in a small town — and must figure out how to break the cycle before he loses his mind.
- *Classic high-concept premise*
- The irony: he's a weatherman. He can't predict what's happening to him.
- "Lose his mind" = stakes (and the real stakes are spiritual, not mental)

**The Shawshank Redemption (1994)**
> A man wrongfully convicted of his wife's murder survives the brutality of prison life through intelligence, quiet determination, and an unbreakable friendship — and a plan no one sees coming.
- *No villain named, stakes implied through character*
- The logline is about the man, not the plot
- "Plan no one sees coming" = the hook

**Arrival (2016)**
> When alien spacecraft land around the world, a linguist is recruited to communicate with the visitors — and as she learns their language, she begins to see time itself differently.
- *The twist is in the logline but sounds like metaphor until you've seen it*
- Stakes: First contact, human survival
- The real story is entirely personal

**Three Billboards Outside Ebbing, Missouri (2017)**
> A grieving mother rents three billboards outside her town to pressure the sheriff into finding her daughter's killer — setting off a chain of violence that forces everyone involved to question what justice really means.
- *Stakes are escalating — "chain of violence"*
- Theme embedded in the logline itself

**Her (2013)**
> A lonely writer in the near future falls in love with his AI operating system — and must reckon with whether that love is real, and whether it matters.
- *The irony is the premise*
- The second sentence does the thematic work

**No Country for Old Men (2007)**
> A hunter stumbles upon a drug deal gone wrong and takes the money — triggering a relentless pursuit by a terrifying hitman that leaves a trail of bodies across West Texas.
- *Clear protagonist, clear antagonist, clear stakes*
- "Relentless pursuit" and "trail of bodies" do the tonal work

**The Hurt Locker (2008)**
> A reckless bomb disposal specialist joins an Army unit in Iraq — and his addiction to the adrenaline of war forces his team to question whether he's protecting them or getting them killed.
- *The conflict is internal externalizing*
- The stakes are both his and theirs

**Spotlight (2015)**
> A team of Boston Globe journalists investigates the systematic cover-up of child abuse by the Catholic Church — and discovers the scandal goes deeper than anyone imagined.
- *Straightforward. The weight of the story carries it.*
- "Deeper than anyone imagined" is the hook

**Moonlight (2016)**
> A young Black man grows up in a rough Miami neighborhood and struggles to find his identity across three chapters of his life — caught between who the world tells him to be and who he truly is.
- *Quiet stakes, deeply personal*
- The logline signals the film's tone and structure
- "Across three chapters" tells us the structure upfront

**Everything Everywhere All at Once (2022)**
> A middle-aged Chinese-American laundromat owner discovers she can access the skills and memories of alternate universe versions of herself — and must use this power to save the multiverse from a nihilistic force threatening all existence.
- *High-concept, sets up the scale*
- The irony: the most mundane protagonist possible with the biggest possible stakes

## The One-Sentence Test

Read your logline out loud. If you have to stop and explain it, it failed. A good logline is self-contained, self-evident, and immediately compelling. It should raise a question that only watching the film can answer.

**The questions a great logline triggers:**
- "How does that happen?"
- "What does that cost them?"
- "How does it end?"
- "I've never seen that before — tell me more."

If someone hears your logline and says "oh interesting, I've seen something like that" — rewrite it.
