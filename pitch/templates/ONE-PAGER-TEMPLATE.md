# One-Pager / Pitch Document Template

The one-pager is the document you send when someone asks for "a little more than the logline but I don't have time to read the script." It's common in producer meetings, pitch situations, and when a manager is shopping your work internally.

One page. Formatted. Looks professional. Reads in 2 minutes.

---

## One-Pager Format

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
                    TITLE
           Genre | Runtime Est. | Written by Your Name
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LOGLINE
[One sentence. Your best version. The hook.]

THE STORY
[Three short paragraphs — no more than 5-6 sentences total.]

Paragraph 1 — Setup:
Establish your protagonist, their world, and what's missing or broken. 
Introduce the inciting event.

Paragraph 2 — Conflict:
The protagonist pursues their goal. The stakes escalate. The midpoint 
complication. The "bad guys close in" pressure.

Paragraph 3 — The Stakes (but NOT the ending):
The all-is-lost moment and what it means. Frame the final confrontation 
without resolving it. Leave them wanting to read the script.

WHY NOW
[1-2 sentences: why this story is timely, culturally resonant, or commercially 
relevant in the current market.]

COMPARABLE FILMS
[Comp 1] meets [Comp 2]

or: In the tradition of [Comp] — [brief positioning statement].

ABOUT THE WRITER
[2-3 sentences: relevant credits, background, why you're the right person 
to write this story.]
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Contact: name@email.com | WGA Registration #XXXXXXXX
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

## Example One-Pager

```
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
                  LAST CALL
        Psychological Thriller | 97 pages | Written by James Moretti
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

LOGLINE
A former FBI hostage negotiator, now working the overnight shift at a 
crisis hotline, receives a call from the man who once held him captive — 
who claims someone is hunting him, and only his old hostage can help.

THE STORY
Marcus Webb was the FBI's best hostage negotiator until the night a 
rescue operation went wrong and a hostage — the woman he loved — died 
on his watch. Three years later, stripped of his badge and his marriage, 
Marcus takes calls on a suicide hotline from midnight to six, one day at 
a time. It's the only work he can still do. Tonight starts like any other.

Then Daniel Harrow calls. The man who held Marcus at gunpoint for six 
days in that failed rescue. Harrow claims he's being hunted — that 
someone is cleaning up loose ends from the operation — and that Marcus 
is the only person who knows enough about that night to help him. Marcus 
has every reason to hang up. He doesn't. As the calls grow more 
desperate, more specific, and more dangerous, Marcus begins to realize 
that Harrow knows things about that night that were never made public.

With each call, Marcus is drawn deeper into a conspiracy that implicates 
people he trusted — and forces him to confront whether the woman he lost 
died by accident or by design. By the time he understands the full shape 
of what happened, the person hunting Harrow has found Marcus too. He has 
one phone and the skills that destroyed him. It'll have to be enough.

WHY NOW
In an era of institutional distrust and the mental health crisis gripping 
first responders, LAST CALL puts a broken system and a broken man in the 
same room for one night — and asks whether either can be redeemed.

COMPARABLE FILMS
Prisoners meets Locke — a confined, psychologically coiled thriller 
driven by performance and escalating revelation.

ABOUT THE WRITER
James Moretti spent four years in crisis intervention before turning to 
writing, and LAST CALL draws directly from that world. His previous 
screenplay was a finalist at the 2024 ScreenCraft Thriller Competition.
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
james@email.com | WGA Reg. #2026-XXXXXX
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
```

## The Comp Film Strategy

Comps ("comparable films") tell the reader where your film lives in the market. They also tell them the approximate budget range and audience.

**Rules for comps:**
- Use two films that together define your genre and tone intersection
- At least one comp should be from the last 5 years (shows market awareness)
- Never comp to the biggest films of all time ("it's like Titanic meets Avengers") — it signals inexperience
- Don't comp to films that flopped
- Don't comp to films so obscure they have to Google them
- The best comp formula: "A meets B" or "In the spirit of A" or "For fans of A and B"

**Good comp framing:**
> "For fans of Prisoners and Zodiac — a methodical, pressure-cooked mystery where the real horror is institutional."

> "In the tradition of A24's elevated horror — The Witch meets Hereditary, but grounded in the specific trauma of first-generation immigration."

> "Get Out meets The Invitation — a social horror that uses genre mechanics to dissect something real."
