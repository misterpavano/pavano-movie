# The Pitch Meeting: How to Walk Into a Room and Sell Your Script

The pitch meeting is the most terrifying and most important skill in a screenwriter's professional life. You've written 110 pages. Now you have 25 minutes to make someone who reads scripts all day want to read yours tonight.

This document covers the verbal pitch — the in-person or Zoom meeting with a producer, manager, development executive, or studio. Not the query letter. The actual room.

---

## Before You Even Get a Meeting

**How pitch meetings happen:**
- A manager requests a meeting after reading your query or a script they were sent
- A contest win or Black List score generates inbound interest
- A referral from another writer, actor, or producer ("you should meet this writer")
- You pitch at a conference (Austin Film Festival, ScreenCraft, etc.)
- A development executive read your script and wants to talk

**You do not cold-call your way into pitch meetings.** You earn them through: great writing, smart querying, contest results, platform (Black List, Stage 32), or network.

---

## Research the Room Before You Walk In

This is not optional. The single most damaging thing you can do in a pitch meeting is waste the executive's time with something obviously wrong for their slate.

**Before every meeting, know:**
- What projects has this company produced or developed recently?
- What genres does this person/company favor?
- What did they pass on publicly that's relevant?
- Any personal information that helps establish human connection (alma mater, previous company, public interviews)?

You're looking for two things: (1) the right way to frame your project for this specific person, and (2) one genuine point of human connection to open with.

## The Pitch Meeting Structure

### Stage 1: Warm the Room (5-10 minutes)

Don't pitch immediately. Every professional who teaches pitching agrees on this: the writer who walks in and immediately starts their prepared pitch is signaling they're nervous and transactional.

Start with conversation. Reference their work. Ask a genuine question about their current slate or how they find projects. Let them talk. People who feel heard make better decisions.

The goal: they like you before they hear a word of your pitch. Because if they like you, they'll work harder to like your script.

**The "why you" opener:**
Once conversation has established some warmth: "Before I get into the project, I wanted to say — the reason I specifically wanted to meet with you is [specific thing about their work that connects to your script]." This tells them you did your homework and you understand their taste.

---

### Stage 2: The Logline Hit (30 seconds)

One sentence. The hook. The logline you've refined until it lands clean.

Deliver it and stop. Let it breathe. Watch their face. Are they leaning in? Do they look confused? Adjust accordingly.

If they immediately ask a question — great. That's engagement. Answer it, then continue.

If they look neutral, continue to the overview.

**Common logline mistakes in the room:**
- Starting with "So, it's kind of like..." (compare at the end, not the beginning)
- Adding "...and then [additional plot detail]" immediately after the logline (you're patching a weak logline in real time)
- Apologizing for the premise ("I know it sounds weird but...")

---

### Stage 3: The Verbal Overview (3-5 minutes)

This is NOT a plot summary. A plot summary is a word-for-word account of what happens. That is boring. Nobody wants to hear the plot of a script summarized linearly.

The verbal overview is a trailer — the most exciting, most emotional, most specific moments of your story, in the order that makes the story feel inevitable.

**Structure of the verbal overview:**
1. **The world** — one sentence. Where and when. What's specific about it.
2. **The protagonist** — one sentence. Who they are, what's broken about them.
3. **The catalyst** — what event throws everything into motion.
4. **The central conflict** — what the protagonist wants and what's standing in the way.
5. **The stakes** — what happens if they fail.
6. **One scene** — describe one specific scene that captures the feeling of your film. Not the whole Act Two. One scene that makes them see the movie in their head.
7. **The ending** — in a pitch, you reveal the ending. Don't be coy. Executives need to know if the story works.

**What to leave out:** Subplots. Secondary character arcs. Specific dialogue. Any plot detail that requires more than one sentence to explain without context.

**The "movie trailer" test:** If what you're saying doesn't make someone want to see the film, cut it from the pitch.

---

### Stage 4: The Characters (1-2 minutes)

After the overview, describe your main characters briefly. Not biographies — the contradiction. What makes each character someone an actor would want to play.

"The protagonist is [X] — outwardly [A], but driven by [B]."

If you're pitching a TV pilot, this section is longer — regular cast members need brief profiles.

**The "dream casting" move:**
In some meetings, pitchers mention dream casting: "I've been thinking about this as something [actor name] would be great for." Do this sparingly and only if it genuinely fits — an obvious, non-aspirational choice ("I was thinking Denzel") reads as desperation. A specific, apt, slightly unexpected choice shows you understand your material.

---

### Stage 5: Comps and Why Now (1 minute)

Two comparable films that together define where your script sits in the market. Deliver them confidently. Then one sentence on why this story is relevant now.

"It sits between *A* and *B* — [how it combines them]. And the reason this story feels timely is [one specific cultural or emotional reason]."

---

### Stage 6: Why You (30 seconds)

Why are you the right person to write this story? Personal connection? Research? Professional background? Lived experience?

This is brief. One or two sentences. But it matters — executives want to know you have genuine investment in this material, not just that you thought it would sell.

---

### Stage 7: The Conversation (as long as they want)

The best pitch meetings stop being pitches and become conversations. The executive starts asking questions. You answer. They start developing the idea in real time with you.

If this happens: great sign. Let them lead. Don't fight to get back to your prepared material. The conversation is the pitch now.

**Questions you'll commonly be asked:**
- "What's the tone?" — have a tight answer. Three adjectives and a comp.
- "Have you thought about who would direct?" — answer honestly if you have an idea; it's fine to say "I've been open about that."
- "What else do you have?" — always have an answer. Two or three other concepts, ready to pitch in one sentence each.
- "Is this based on anything?" — if it's original, say so. If it's based on existing IP, have the rights situation ready to discuss.
- "Where are you in the writing?" — be honest. "I have a completed draft" is better than "I'm still outlining."

---

### Stage 8: The Exit (2 minutes)

Don't press for a decision in the room. Don't ask "so, do you think you'd want to read it?" They'll tell you. If they want to read it, they'll say "can you send it over?" right there.

What you say at the end: "Thank you for your time — I've been looking forward to this conversation." That's it. Get out cleanly.

**The follow-up email:**
Send within 24 hours. Two sentences: "Great meeting you today — really enjoyed the conversation about [specific thing]. Happy to send the script over whenever works for you." Don't summarize the pitch in the email. Don't add new information. Just establish the connection and leave the door open.

## Pitching for TV (The Series Pitch)

TV pitches are structurally different from feature pitches. You're not selling a script — you're selling a world that generates seasons of television.

**What a TV pitch covers (in order):**
1. Logline for the series
2. The world (specific, textured, generative)
3. The protagonist and their wound (this is what sustains a series — a wound that takes seasons to address)
4. The series engine (what generates conflict week after week)
5. Season one arc: where do we start, where do we end, what's the season one finale?
6. Two or three episode springboards (brief examples of the engine in action)
7. Future seasons (1 sentence each for seasons 2 and 3)
8. Why you for this story

TV pitches are often done with a leave-behind: a printed series bible or pitch deck. Some writers use visual boards (tone images, location references, character reference photos). This is optional for features; increasingly expected for TV.

## Pitch Mistakes to Avoid

**Coming in too hot.** Walking in and immediately pitching before any human connection is established is the single most damaging thing. You'll be categorized as a transactional writer, not a collaborator.

**Summarizing the plot linearly.** "And then this happens, and then this happens, and then..." The executive's eyes will close. Pitch the feeling, not the plot.

**Being defensive about notes.** If they offer a suggestion or challenge, don't defend your choices. Say "that's interesting — tell me more." Even if you disagree. The meeting is not the time to fight for your vision. The meeting is for making them feel heard and excited.

**Not knowing the ending.** If you pitch without knowing how your script ends, you don't know your script well enough to pitch it.

**Over-preparing to the point of rigidity.** Rehearse until you're confident, not until you're reciting. A rehearsed pitch that leaves no room for conversation is a monologue, not a meeting.

**Pitching projects that don't fit the room.** If you know they make only micro-budget horror and you're pitching a $100M action film, you've wasted both your time and theirs.

## The Follow-Up After a Full Request

They asked to read it. You sent it.

**Standard response time:** 4-6 weeks. This is not a publishing house — it can move faster. But 4-6 weeks is normal.

**One follow-up:** After 6 weeks of silence, a single brief email: "Just checking in on [TITLE] — let me know if you need anything from my end."

**If they pass:** "Thanks so much for reading — I appreciate your time. Would love to stay in touch as I develop new projects." Move on. A pass is not personal. It means it wasn't right for their slate right now.

**If they want to meet:** Go back in with any notes they mentioned, and with your next project ready to discuss.
