# Query Letter Template

The query letter is how you get a manager or production company to read your script. It is not a cover letter. It is not a resume. It is a sales pitch written to make a busy professional stop scrolling and say "send it."

Most query letters are terrible. Most scripts never get read because the query letter killed them. Don't be most writers.

---

## The Rules Before You Write a Word

**Rule 1: One page maximum. Always.**
If your query is longer than one page, it goes in the trash.

**Rule 2: They're not looking for reasons to say yes — they're looking for reasons to say no.**
Every vague word, every cliché, every grammar error is a reason. Give them nothing to hold against you.

**Rule 3: The logline does 80% of the work.**
If your logline is weak, no amount of bio or flattery saves you.

**Rule 4: Personalize every letter.**
"Dear Manager" goes in the trash. Know who you're writing to. Know what they've worked on. Say why you're specifically reaching out to them.

**Rule 5: Email, not snail mail.**
It's 2025. The industry runs on email. Subject line matters — see below.

**Rule 6: Never attach the script unsolicited.**
You're requesting permission to send the script. Including it uninvited is a red flag.

## The Structure

### Subject Line
The subject line is the first test. It should be professional, clear, and intriguing — not desperate.

**Good:**
> Query: [TITLE] — [GENRE], [PAGE COUNT]
> Example: `Query: LAST EXIT — Contained Thriller, 104 pages`

**Bad:**
> - "This is the next Die Hard!!!"
> - "screenplay I've been working on"
> - "URGENT — please read"
> - "Collaboration opportunity"

---

### Opening Paragraph — The Hook (2-3 sentences)

Lead with your logline. Not "I'm a writer who..." Not "I've always loved movies since..." The logline first.

Then — ONE sentence on why you're reaching out to this specific person. Reference a film they produced or a client they manage. Show you did your homework.

**Template:**
```
[LOGLINE — polished, punchy, one sentence.]

I'm reaching out because of your work on [specific project] — I think [TITLE] might be a fit for your slate.
```

**Example:**
```
A former hostage negotiator, now blackout-drunk and working the overnight shift at a crisis hotline, receives a call from the man who held him captive — who claims someone is hunting him and only his old hostage can help.

I'm reaching out because of your work on Prisoners and Prisoners of the Ghostland — I believe LAST CALL has the kind of tightly-coiled, psychologically complex tension that's central to your slate.
```

---

### Second Paragraph — The Synopsis (3-5 sentences)

Expand the logline into a brief synopsis. Hit the major structural moments — setup, escalation, stakes. Do NOT summarize the entire script. Do NOT reveal the ending. Leave them wanting to read it.

**Template:**
```
[TITLE] is a [GENRE] set in [WORLD/SETTING]. [Hero] is [setup + defining situation]. When [catalyst], [hero] must [goal], but [antagonistic force] stands in the way. As [escalating complication], [hero] faces [ultimate stakes].
```

**Example:**
```
LAST CALL is a contained psychological thriller set over a single night. Marcus Webb, once the FBI's best hostage negotiator, lost everything after a failed negotiation cost two lives — including the hostage he loved. Now he takes calls on a suicide hotline, three years sober until tonight. When a caller claims to be Daniel Harrow — the man who held Marcus at gunpoint for six days — and says that someone is hunting him for something he did, Marcus has to decide whether to believe him, help him, or hang up. As the night stretches on and the calls become more desperate, Marcus uncovers a conspiracy that reaches into his own past — and realizes that whether or not Harrow is telling the truth, someone is listening to every word.
```

---

### Third Paragraph — Your Credentials (2-3 sentences)

This is the paragraph most first-time writers get wrong. They either apologize for not having credits, or they list unrelated accomplishments that don't matter.

**If you have credits:**
List them. Simple. "My previous script TITLE placed in the top 10% of the Nicholl Fellowship and was optioned by [producer]."

**If you have no credits:**
Lead with what you have — contest placements, professional background relevant to the story, or just keep it brief and confident.

**If you have nothing:**
Keep it to one sentence. Don't apologize. Don't fill space. Acknowledge and move on.

**Templates:**

*With credits:*
```
My screenplay [TITLE] placed in the top 10% of the [YEAR] Academy Nicholl Fellowship and was a finalist at the Austin Film Festival. I am repped by [manager/agent] for [other work].
```

*With contest placements only:*
```
LAST CALL placed in the Top 25 of the 2024 ScreenCraft Thriller Competition and received a score of 8/10 on The Black List. I'm a former crisis counselor, and the world of the script draws directly from that experience.
```

*With relevant professional background:*
```
I spent twelve years as a criminal defense attorney, and LAST CALL is informed by a decade of watching negotiations — legal and otherwise — go wrong. This is my fourth completed feature screenplay.
```

*With nothing:*
```
This is my third completed feature screenplay. I have no previous credits.
```

The last one works because confidence reads better than hedging. "I hope you might possibly consider" is weaker than the absence of apology.

---

### Closing — The Ask (1-2 sentences)

Make the ask clear. You want them to request the full screenplay. Keep it professional.

**Template:**
```
I would be happy to send the full screenplay at your request. Thank you for your time and consideration.
```

Or:
```
The full script is available upon request. I look forward to hearing from you.
```

Do NOT say:
- "I know you're busy" (they know they're busy)
- "I'm sure you get tons of these" (you're preemptively lowering your status)
- "Feel free to pass if it's not right for you" (you're telling them it's okay to say no)

---

## The Full Template

```
Subject: Query: [TITLE] — [Genre], [Page count]

Dear [Name],

[LOGLINE — one sentence, polished.]

I'm reaching out because of your work on [specific project] — I believe [TITLE] might resonate with your slate.

[TITLE] is a [genre] set in [world]. [SETUP in 1-2 sentences]. When [catalyst], [hero] must [goal]. But [antagonistic complication]. [Escalating stakes in 1 sentence].

[CREDENTIALS — 1-3 sentences, honest, no apologies.]

The full screenplay is available upon request. Thank you for your time.

[Your name]
[Your email]
[Your phone — optional]
```

## Full Example Letter

```
Subject: Query: LAST CALL — Psychological Thriller, 97 pages

Dear Sarah,

A former FBI hostage negotiator, now working the overnight shift at a crisis hotline, receives a call from the man who once held him captive — who claims someone is hunting him, and only his old hostage can help.

I'm reaching out because of your work on Prisoners — I think LAST CALL shares its DNA: a contained, psychologically complex story where the threat is as much internal as external.

LAST CALL is a psychological thriller set over a single night in a crisis call center. Marcus Webb was the best negotiator the FBI had — until a hostage died on his watch, along with his career and his marriage. Now three years sober, he takes calls from people at the edge. When Daniel Harrow calls — the man who held Marcus at gunpoint for six days in a failed rescue operation — Marcus has to decide whether to believe him, help him, or hang up. As the calls grow more desperate and more dangerous, Marcus begins to uncover a conspiracy that reaches into his own past, and realizes the only way to get off this call alive is to use every skill that destroyed him.

LAST CALL was a finalist in the 2024 ScreenCraft Thriller Competition. I spent four years working crisis intervention before transitioning to writing, and this script draws from that world directly.

The full screenplay is available upon request. Thank you for your time.

James Moretti
james@email.com
```

## Who to Send It To

See `research/market/WHO-TO-PITCH.md` for the full list of managers, production companies, and submission portals.

**General rules:**
- Query literary managers first, not agents (managers develop; agents deal)
- Send to 10-15 simultaneously, track them in `submissions/TRACKER.md`
- Wait 4-6 weeks before following up once
- If no response after one follow-up, move on

## After You Send It

**If they request the script:**
Send it immediately. No cover letter needed — they know what it is. PDF format. Final Draft-style formatting. Filename: `TITLE_LASTNAME.pdf`.

**If they pass:**
"Thank you for reading. I'd love to keep you in mind for future projects." Move on.

**If you hear nothing:**
After 4-6 weeks, one follow-up: "I wanted to follow up on my query for LAST CALL sent [date]. Happy to resend if it went astray." If nothing after that, they passed.
