# Treatment Template

A treatment is a scene-by-scene (or sequence-by-sequence) summary of your entire script, written in present tense, in prose. It reads like a short story version of your film.

Treatments are requested when a producer or executive wants to evaluate your story before committing to read the full script — or when you're being hired to write something and they need to approve the direction before you write pages.

**Length:** 10-20 pages for a feature (scene-by-scene). 3-5 pages for a short synopsis treatment (sequence-by-sequence). Know which one they're asking for.

---

## When You Need a Treatment

- A producer has expressed interest and asks for "something to share with my partner before I read the full script"
- You're pitching an original spec and they want to make sure the story works before reading 110 pages
- You're being hired on assignment and the studio needs to approve the story before you write
- Development executives use treatments for internal greenlight discussions
- Some managers use treatments to pitch their clients' projects to studios verbally

---

## Treatment Rules

**Write in present tense, third person:**
Not "John walked to the door" — "John walks to the door."

**Write in active, visual prose:**
This is not a novel. Write what we SEE and HEAR. Not what characters think or feel unless it's expressed through action.

**Don't write dialogue:**
A few key lines are fine, but a treatment full of dialogue reads like a draft screenplay. Keep it scene summary.

**Move fast:**
A treatment that takes 20 pages to get to the inciting incident has a pacing problem — in the script too, probably. Get in, hit the beats, get out.

**Reveal the ending:**
Unlike the one-pager, the treatment shows everything. The whole story. Don't hold back the ending — the producer needs to know if the story works.

**Maintain the feeling:**
A good treatment is not a dry outline. It should make the reader feel the genre. An action treatment reads fast. A horror treatment should feel unsettling. Don't write it in a neutral vacuum.

## Treatment Format

### Header
```
TITLE
Written by [Name]
[Genre] | Approximately [page count] pages

WGA Registration #XXXXXXXX
Contact: name@email.com
```

### Opening (1-2 paragraphs)
Set the world, tone, and protagonist. Like a novel's opening. Make us feel the world before the story begins. This is your only chance for atmosphere — use it.

### Act One (2-4 pages)
- The protagonist in their ordinary world
- The inciting incident
- The decision to engage (or forced engagement)
- Clear establishment of the story's central conflict

### Act Two (5-10 pages)
- The protagonist's pursuit of their goal
- Key obstacles and reversals
- Major set pieces (described cinematically)
- The midpoint shift
- The escalating pressure
- The All Is Lost moment

### Act Three (2-4 pages)
- The hero's turn — the insight or decision that enables the finale
- The climax
- Resolution of all major storylines
- The final image

## Example Treatment Opening (Full Page)

```
LAST CALL

Written by James Moretti
Psychological Thriller | Approximately 97 pages

WGA Registration #2026-XXXXXX
james@email.com

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The crisis hotline operates out of a converted office building in 
downtown Baltimore. At night, the building is mostly dark — except 
for one floor, where a row of cubicles stays lit until sunrise. 
The callers come in from the dark. The counselors listen.

MARCUS WEBB, 42, sits in his cubicle with the lights off. He 
answers calls in the dark, the glow of his monitor the only light. 
He has the voice for it — calm, measured, low — but his hands 
shake sometimes and he drinks too much coffee and the photograph 
of a woman that used to be on his desk is gone, replaced by a 
rubber band and a chip from AA that he touches like a rosary.

Three years ago, Marcus was the FBI's best hostage negotiator. 
Three years ago, a rescue operation in a warehouse outside Baltimore 
went wrong. Two people died: a hostage named CLAIRE REEVES, and 
the version of Marcus Webb who believed the job was worth it.

Tonight, Marcus takes the midnight shift. The calls come in: a 
veteran who can't sleep, a college student who swallowed something 
and isn't sure she wants to say what, a man who just needs to hear 
someone's voice. Marcus handles them all with the quiet competence 
of someone who has been broken down to what's essential. He is 
good at this because he has nothing left to protect.

At 2:47 AM, a call comes in with a number the system flags as 
blocked. Marcus answers anyway.

"Is this Marcus Webb?"

No one at this number knows his name.

"Yes," says Marcus. "Who is this?"

"You know who this is," the caller says. "You spent six days in 
a room with me. You never forget a voice."

Marcus goes very still.

The caller is DANIEL HARROW. The man who held Marcus at gunpoint 
for six days during the Claire Reeves rescue — the man who Marcus 
was negotiating with when she died.

Harrow says he needs help. He says someone is hunting him, cleaning 
up witnesses from that night. He says he has information about what 
really happened to Claire — information that was never in any report. 
And he says Marcus is the only person who would know whether he's 
telling the truth.

Marcus should hang up.

He doesn't.
```

```
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ACT TWO

[Early second act — the hero in the new world, early gains and setbacks.]

[Midpoint — the major reversal or revelation. Everything shifts here.]

[Bad guys close in — escalating pressure from all sides. What's fracturing?]

[The dark moment — the lowest point. What dies?]

─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

ACT THREE

[The turn — the insight or choice that enables the finale.]

[The climax — the final confrontation. How does the hero's arc provide 
the solution? Both external conflict and internal arc resolved.]

[Resolution — what is the new normal? How is the world (and the hero) 
different?]

[Final image — the visual proof of transformation.]
```

## Short Synopsis Version (3-5 pages)

For informal requests or early-stage pitching, a 3-5 page synopsis treatment works:
- One paragraph per act beat (not per scene)
- Total of 8-10 paragraphs
- Hits every major structural moment
- Includes the ending
- Same present tense, active prose rules apply

Use the Three-Act Beat Sheet (`notes/beat-sheets/THREE-ACT-BEAT-SHEET.md`) as your structural backbone and write one paragraph per beat.
