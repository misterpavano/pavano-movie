# Character

What separates a character audiences remember from one they forget by the parking lot.

The answer isn't complexity for its own sake. It isn't backstory. It isn't "making them likable." It's **contradiction** — a character who wants one thing but needs another, and whose flaw is the exact thing standing between them and what they need. That tension, lived out across 110 pages, is what makes a human being feel real.

---

## Want vs. Need: The Central Engine

Every protagonist has a **want** and a **need**. They are different. They are often in conflict.

**Want:** The external goal the protagonist is consciously pursuing. What they think will make them happy. What the plot is about.

**Need:** What the protagonist actually requires to be whole. The internal wound that must heal. What the movie is really about.

The protagonist usually achieves what they need by the end — sometimes at the cost of the want, sometimes by realizing they already had it.

### Examples

| Film | Want | Need |
|---|---|---|
| *Rocky* | To win the fight | To prove to himself he's not a bum |
| *Legally Blonde* | To get Warner back | To value herself without a man's approval |
| *Iron Man* | To escape captivity / be seen as cool | To become genuinely responsible for others |
| *The Social Network* | To build something great / impress his ex | To connect with people, to be genuinely liked |
| *Inside Out* | Riley wants to fit in in San Francisco | Riley needs to let herself feel sadness, not just joy |
| *Chinatown* | Gittes wants to solve the case | Gittes needs to accept that he can't save everyone |

### How to Find Your Character's Want and Need

Ask these questions:
1. What does your protagonist say they want in the first 10 pages?
2. What do they NEVER talk about? (That's the wound)
3. What belief do they hold that's causing them suffering?
4. What would they have to give up / admit / accept to be truly happy?
5. What would happen if they got everything they wanted — would they actually be happy?

If the answer to #5 is "no" — you've found the gap between want and need. That gap is your story.

## Character Arc Types

### The Positive Arc (Growth Arc)
The most common arc in commercial storytelling. The protagonist has a flawed worldview at the start. By the end, they've shed the lie and embraced a truth.

*Setup:* Character believes a lie about themselves or the world.
*Journey:* Events force them to confront the lie repeatedly. They resist.
*Climax:* They choose the truth, even at great cost.
*Resolution:* The world reflects the change.

_Example:_ Walter White's inverse — or rather, *Schindler's List*. Schindler begins as a self-interested opportunist who sees Jews as cheap labor. By the end, he's spending everything he has to save lives.

### The Negative Arc (Fall Arc)
The protagonist starts with the truth within reach but chooses the lie. They spiral into destruction.

*Setup:* Character is warned. They have a chance to change.
*Journey:* They choose their flaw over and over, escalating consequences.
*Climax:* The final, tragic choice cements their fate.

_Example:_ Michael Corleone in *The Godfather*. He starts as the one son who escaped the family business. He ends as the most ruthless of all of them.

### The Flat Arc (Steadfast Arc)
The protagonist's worldview doesn't change — but the world around them does. Often used for mentor figures, genre heroes, or characters who represent an ideal.

*Setup:* Character holds a true belief in a corrupt world.
*Journey:* The world tests that belief, tries to bend them.
*Climax:* The character refuses to break. Their truth infects the world.

_Example:* Atticus Finch in *To Kill a Mockingbird*. He doesn't change. His moral clarity is the lens through which the world's injustice is revealed.

## Ghost and Wound

The Ghost is the traumatic event (or series of events) in the protagonist's past that created their wound. The Wound is the emotional scar that shapes every major decision they make.

You don't need to show the Ghost on screen. In fact, great writing often never shows it directly. But YOU need to know it. It explains everything.

**The Wound → The Lie → The Flaw cycle:**

1. Something painful happened (the Ghost)
2. The protagonist drew a conclusion about themselves or the world (the Lie)
3. They built their personality around that Lie as a defense mechanism (the Flaw)
4. The Flaw keeps getting in the way of what they need

_Example:_
- Ghost: As a child, Tony Stark watched his father give more attention to work than to him. Howard Stark was cold, distant, impossible to please.
- Lie: "I'm only worth loving if I achieve something. Love has to be earned."
- Flaw: Narcissism, inability to form genuine connections, need to be the smartest person in the room.
- What he needs to learn: genuine self-sacrifice, responsibility that doesn't require applause.

The arc of *Iron Man* is Tony learning that "I am Iron Man" isn't about ego — it's about taking actual responsibility.

### Finding Your Character's Ghost

Ask:
- What's the worst thing that ever happened to them before page 1?
- What false conclusion did they draw from that event?
- How does that false conclusion show up in every major decision?
- What would they NEVER admit out loud?
- What are they most afraid of people finding out about them?

## The Antagonist as Protagonist Mirror

Weak antagonists want to destroy things because they're evil. Strong antagonists want exactly what the protagonist wants — and represent what the protagonist could become if they follow their flaw to its conclusion.

The antagonist is a dark mirror. They force the protagonist to confront the logical endpoint of their own flaw.

**The mirror questions:**
- What does the antagonist want?
- Is it the same thing the protagonist wants?
- Why is the antagonist pursuing it the wrong way?
- How is the protagonist at risk of becoming the antagonist?

_Example:_
- In *The Dark Knight*, the Joker wants freedom from civilization's rules. Batman also operates outside the law. The Joker is what Batman looks like without moral restraint.
- In *Whiplash*, Fletcher believes suffering creates greatness. Andrew believes the same thing — that's why he can't quit. Fletcher is Andrew's possible future.
- In *Black Swan*, Nina's dark self IS her antagonist. Her need for perfection destroys her.

The antagonist doesn't have to be a person. It can be a system, a force, a disease, a weather pattern. But it must represent the external manifestation of the protagonist's internal flaw.

## Subtext in Dialogue

Characters never say what they mean. People don't talk that way. Real human speech is full of avoidance, deflection, performance, and half-truth.

Subtext is what the character means under what they say.

**On the nose (bad):**
> JAMES: I feel like you don't respect me. It makes me feel like I'm not good enough, and I think that comes from how my father treated me.

**Subtext-driven (good):**
> JAMES: You want the window seat?
> SARAH: No, I'm fine.
> JAMES: You're sure?
> SARAH: I said I'm fine, James.
> *(She takes the window seat.)*

The second version says something about power dynamics, resentment, and unspoken grievance — without saying any of those words.

**How to write subtext:**
1. Know what your character actually wants from this scene
2. Know why they can't say it directly
3. Have them ask for something else while wanting that thing
4. Let their ACTIONS betray what their words deny

The gold standard: the audience understands what the character means even though the character never says it.

## Distinct Character Voices

Every character should sound different. If you cover the character names and read the dialogue aloud, you should be able to tell who's talking.

Voice differentiation comes from:

**Vocabulary:** 
A Harvard professor uses different words than a 17-year-old from Queens. Not just vocabulary level — rhythm, reference, slang, precision.

**Sentence structure:**
Some characters speak in fragments. Others in complete, structured paragraphs. Some repeat themselves. Some never finish a sentence.

**What they talk about:**
A character obsessed with money will find financial metaphors in every situation. A former athlete will reference sports constantly.

**What they won't talk about:**
What a character avoids reveals as much as what they engage with.

**Rhythm and pace:**
Fast talkers, slow talkers, interrupters, listeners. These rhythms should feel distinct on the page.

**The test:** Print a 2-page scene. Cover all character names. Read it. Can you identify each speaker? If not, voices aren't differentiated enough.

## Secondary Characters Serving Theme

Secondary characters aren't decoration. Every significant secondary character should:

1. **Challenge the protagonist's belief system** — they represent an alternate answer to the story's thematic question
2. **Reveal aspects of the protagonist** the protagonist can't see themselves
3. **Advance the plot** through their own desires (not just to assist the hero)
4. **Have their own want** — secondary characters who exist only to help the hero are props, not people

### The Thematic Spectrum

In well-constructed films, secondary characters often represent different positions on the thematic question.

_Example from Tootsie:_
- The thematic question: What does it mean to respect women?
- Sandy: represents men who are verbally "feminist" but behaviorally dismissive
- Jeff (the roommate): the male gaze made articulate — pure honesty about how men think
- Julie: represents what Michael/Dorothy is learning to understand
- Ron: represents what Michael was before — a man who performs sensitivity while actually being controlling

Each secondary character embodies a different answer to the same question. The protagonist's journey is deciding which answer is true.

## The Save-the-Cat Moment for Every Major Character

Blake Snyder's principle applies to ALL significant characters, not just the protagonist.

Every character the audience spends significant time with needs a moment early on that earns the audience's investment. It doesn't have to be positive — it just has to be human and specific.

For a villain: show us something they value, even if they pursue it through monstrous means. Hannibal Lecter has impeccable taste and a deep appreciation for art. We find that fascinating even as we're horrified. His save-the-cat moment is the elegance of his mind.

For a secondary antagonist: show us their wound. Even minor antagonists become interesting when we understand what made them this way.

For the protagonist's love interest: give them their own problem. Show us that they're a full person before the protagonist enters their life. Nothing is worse than a love interest who exists only to be won.

**The test:** If a secondary character disappeared from the story, would you miss them? Would the protagonist be less tested, less revealed, less challenged? If not, the character needs work.
