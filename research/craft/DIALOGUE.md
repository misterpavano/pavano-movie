# Dialogue

The hardest craft skill in screenwriting, and the most immediately revealing.

Bad dialogue announces itself on the first read. On-the-nose exposition, characters who sound identical, speeches that exist to tell the audience how to feel — these problems scream from the page. Readers pass. Producers pass. Actors pass.

The good news: dialogue problems are fixable. The fixes are learnable.

---

## Subtext vs. On-the-Nose

This is the most fundamental distinction in dialogue craft.

**On-the-nose:** Characters say exactly what they mean. The dialogue is doing the work of explaining itself.

**Subtext-driven:** Characters talk around what they mean. The real meaning lives under the words.

### Why People Don't Say What They Mean

In real life, people avoid direct statements because:
- They're afraid of rejection
- They're ashamed of what they actually want
- They don't know how to say it
- The social situation makes directness impossible
- They're lying
- They're testing the other person
- They're performing a version of themselves
- They want deniability

Your characters should have the same problem.

### The Test

Read your dialogue aloud and ask: "Is there any way a real person would actually say these words in this situation?" If the answer is no — if it only exists because the plot needs the information delivered — it's on the nose.

**On the nose example:**
> RACHEL: I've been feeling like ever since your promotion, you've been treating me differently. Like I'm less important to you. I think it's because you're scared of commitment and your father was emotionally unavailable.

Nobody talks like this. This is therapy-speak dropped into drama.

**Subtext-driven version:**
> RACHEL: You made the reservations for the 28th.
> MARK: Yeah?
> RACHEL: Our anniversary is the 27th.
> MARK: I know when our anniversary is.
> RACHEL: *(beat)* Good. Just making sure.

The conflict is real, the resentment is present, the dynamic is revealed — and none of it is said directly. This is how people actually fight.

## Exposition Without It Feeling Like Exposition

Exposition is information the audience needs to understand the story. The problem: whenever a character explains something for the benefit of the audience, it sounds artificial.

The challenge: you have to get information across. The craft: making it feel natural.

### Techniques

**Give the exposition to someone who doesn't want to receive it.**
If Character A needs to tell Character B something, it lands better if B is distracted, resistant, or trying to leave. The interruption makes the exposition feel incidental rather than deliberate.

**Bury it in conflict.**
A conversation about a character's backstory lands differently when it's also an argument. The information is still delivered, but the emotional content distracts from the mechanics.

**Dramatize it, don't explain it.**
Instead of having a character say "I've always been afraid of commitment since my first marriage fell apart," show us a moment where that fear manifests. Let the behavior carry the information.

**Use the professional context.**
Certain jobs require exposition — doctors explain diagnoses, detectives brief colleagues, teachers instruct students. Leverage these contexts where explanation is natural.

**"As you know, Bob..."**
This is the classic exposition mistake: two characters explaining things to each other that both of them already know, purely for the audience's benefit. Never do this.

*Bad:*
> DETECTIVE: As you know, Bob, we've been tracking this suspect for two years since the Riverside murders.
> BOB: Yes, and as you know, John, the suspect has eluded us at every turn.

*Better:*
> DETECTIVE: *(reading a file)* Two years. And he's still walking around.
> BOB: We'll get him.
> DETECTIVE: That's what you said after Riverside.

## The 5 Functions of Dialogue

Every line of dialogue should be doing at least one of these things. If it's not doing any of them, cut it.

### 1. Advance the Plot
The line moves the story forward. A decision is made. A fact is revealed. A confrontation begins.

### 2. Reveal Character
The line shows us who this person is — their values, fears, obsessions, class, intelligence, humor. Character is revealed not just by what people say but by how they say it, what they choose to mention, and what they conspicuously avoid.

### 3. Establish or Shift Relationship
Power dynamics, affection, resentment, familiarity — these are established and shifted through dialogue. Every time two characters talk, their relationship should be changed (even slightly) by the end of the scene.

### 4. Plant or Pay Off Information
A line sets up something that will matter later (plant) or delivers the payoff of something planted earlier. Good scripts are full of these. Audiences don't always notice them consciously, but they feel the satisfaction.

### 5. Create Tone / Atmosphere
Some lines exist to establish the world's emotional texture — its humor, its dread, its rhythm. A particularly sharp piece of banter can tell us more about the world than three pages of description.

**The test:** Go through your script line by line. For each line, ask: which of these 5 functions is it serving? If the answer is "none," cut it.

## How People Actually Talk

Most beginning writers write dialogue that is too complete, too logical, and too on-message. Real speech is messy.

### People interrupt.
> SARAH: I think we should just —
> JAMES: No. I've been thinking about this and —
> SARAH: You never let me —
> JAMES: Because every time I do —

### People don't answer the question asked.
> DETECTIVE: Where were you on Friday night?
> EVAN: *(long pause)* You know, I had this professor once — brilliant guy — who said that the truth is just the story that nobody can disprove.
> DETECTIVE: Where were you Friday night, Evan.

The deflection tells us more than an answer would.

### People trail off.
Ellipses (...) in screenplay dialogue should be used sparingly — but they're real. People lose their train of thought. They decide not to finish what they started. They let silence do the work.

### People repeat themselves.
Especially under stress or when they're not being heard. This is a character trait worth building into specific characters: some people circle when anxious.

### People use filler.
Real speech is full of "I mean," "you know," "like," "kind of." Use this sparingly in dialogue — read enough of it and it becomes tedious — but a carefully placed verbal tic can make a character leap off the page.

### People lie.
A lot. They lie to protect themselves, to protect others, to avoid confrontation, to perform a version of themselves. If your characters almost never lie, they don't sound human.

## Silences as Dialogue

The pause is one of the most powerful tools in the dialogue writer's kit. Used correctly, a beat of silence conveys more than a page of text.

**When to use silence:**

- After a devastating revelation
- When a character decides not to say something
- When a character realizes something they don't want to admit
- When the power in a scene shifts
- When words aren't enough

**How to write it in Fountain:**

```fountain
SARAH
You knew. You knew the whole time.

*(beat)*

MARK
I told myself it was different.
```

The `*(beat)*` is your silence. Use parentheticals for brief pauses; action lines for longer ones.

```fountain
SARAH
You knew. You knew the whole time.

Mark doesn't answer. He walks to the window and stares at 
the street below. When he finally turns back, something 
has closed in his face.

MARK
I told myself it was different.
```

### The Unfinished Scene

Some of the most powerful dialogue scenes end before the obvious conclusion. The characters don't resolve their conflict. They stop. The audience fills in the rest.

This is called "leaving on the turn" — ending the scene at the moment of shift, not after it's been processed.

## Voice Differentiation

If you cover the character names and can't tell who's speaking, the voices need work.

**Build a voice dossier for each character before you write them:**

- **Education level and background:** Shapes vocabulary, syntax, reference points
- **What they're passionate about:** Shapes metaphor and what they notice
- **What they're afraid of:** Shapes what they avoid saying
- **How they handle conflict:** Attack? Deflect? Go cold?
- **Their verbal habit:** Do they qualify everything? Speak in absolutes? Ask questions? Issue commands?
- **Their rhythm:** Short sentences or long ones? Do they let silence in or fill it?

### Voice Examples from the Same Setup

The setup: Someone just told them their project failed.

**Character A** (former soldier, direct, low-emotion):
> That's not ideal.
> *(beat)*
> What do we do now?

**Character B** (academic, needs to process everything through analysis):
> I mean, that's — okay. That's data. That's just data we didn't have before. The question is what it tells us about the hypothesis, which I'd argue is still —

**Character C** (immigrant who learned English as a second language, formal):
> I see. Thank you for informing me directly. I will need some time to consider the implications.

**Character D** (young, anxious, self-deprecating):
> Okay, yeah, no. That's — yeah. That tracks. That's basically what I expected, honestly, so it's not even... I mean, it's fine. It's totally fine.

Same information. Four completely different people revealed.

**The read-aloud test:** Print your script and read each character's lines aloud in one block. Does each character have a consistent, identifiable voice? Can you hear the difference?
