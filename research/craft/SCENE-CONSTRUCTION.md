# Scene Construction

A screenplay is a sequence of scenes. Each scene is a unit of drama with its own internal logic. Getting scenes right is getting the script right.

---

## Enter Late, Leave Early

The most important tactical rule in scene construction.

Every scene has a natural beginning, middle, and end. You don't need to show all three. **Enter the scene as late as possible.** Cut before the natural ending.

This does several things:
- Eliminates small talk and pleasantries that dilute dramatic tension
- Creates forward momentum (the audience is always slightly behind, always catching up)
- Trusts the audience to fill in what they missed
- Makes scenes feel urgent

**The test:** Identify the moment when the scene's conflict becomes undeniable. Start 30 seconds before that. Everything before it is setup the audience doesn't need.

**Example:**
A scene where a detective confronts a suspect.

*Bad entry:*
> The detective WALKS DOWN A HALL. He stops at a door. He knocks. A WOMAN opens it. She looks nervous.
> DETECTIVE: Ms. Hartley? I'm Detective Reyes. I'm sorry to bother you this evening.
> MS. HARTLEY: How can I help you?

*Good entry:*
> Detective Reyes is already in the apartment. Ms. Hartley perches on the edge of a chair — nowhere to go.
> DETECTIVE: You didn't mention you knew him.

The scene is already in conflict. The audience catches up immediately.

## Scene Goal, Conflict, Outcome

Every scene needs three things:

**Goal:** What does the protagonist want at the start of this scene? Be specific. Not "she wants to get information" but "she wants to know if he was in Phoenix on the 12th." The more specific the goal, the more specific the scene.

**Conflict:** What stands between the protagonist and their goal? The conflict doesn't have to be physical. It can be:
- Another character who wants the opposite thing
- An obstacle that makes the goal impossible
- The protagonist's own flaw
- A secret that changes the stakes
- Time pressure

**Outcome:** Does the protagonist get what they wanted? In strong scripts, the outcome is rarely a clean yes or no. It's a Yes-But or a No-And.

---

## Scene Turns: Yes-But / No-And

The most important concept in scene construction. Developed by story structure expert John Truby and widely used in professional script development.

**A scene must end differently than it began.**

This is the "turn" — the value shift. Something changes in the scene. If you could remove the scene without changing anything about the story, the scene shouldn't be there.

The most powerful outcomes:

**Yes-But:** The protagonist gets what they want, but with a complication that creates a new problem.
> She gets the information she needed — but now the informant wants something dangerous in return.

**No-And:** The protagonist doesn't get what they want, and it's worse than before.
> He doesn't get the job — and the rejection makes him do something reckless.

Avoid **Yes** (clean victory, no complication) and **No** (clean defeat, no escalation) — they kill momentum.

**The turn test:** What is the value (hope/despair, safety/danger, connection/isolation) at the start of the scene? What is it at the end? If the value hasn't shifted, the scene has no turn and should be rethought.

## Visual Storytelling

Screenwriting is writing for the eye. The camera sees behavior, not psychology. You cannot write "SARAH realizes she's been wrong all along." You can write what Sarah does when she realizes that.

**Show, don't tell — but specifically:**

The cliché "show, don't tell" is true but useless without specificity. Here's how:

**Behavior over statement:**
Don't write "JAMES is nervous." Write what nervousness looks like: "JAMES checks his phone. Puts it down. Picks it up again. There are no new messages."

**Objects carry meaning:**
Give characters objects that reveal their inner life. A character who keeps a photo of their dead spouse. A character who always has a flask. A character who straightens every crooked picture they pass. Objects are your shorthand for character.

**The environment reflects the character:**
The state of a character's apartment tells us who they are. Bare walls and unpacked boxes: they haven't committed to being here. A wall covered in obsessively detailed notes: the character we're about to meet is either brilliant or unwell.

**Contrast creates meaning:**
A luxury penthouse. A sleeping bag in the corner. We know everything about this person's priority set before they say a word.

**The telling detail:**
One specific, exact detail does more work than ten general ones. Not "an old car" — "a '92 Civic with a Subway loyalty card stuck in the visor and enough fast food bags in the back to carbon-date." Now we know who drives this car.

## Action Line Style

Action lines are the description in your script. They should be:

**Present tense:** Everything is happening now.
> Sarah RUNS down the hall. Not: Sarah ran down the hall.

**Active voice:**
> The door SWINGS open. Not: The door is swung open.

**Short paragraphs:**
No action block should be longer than 4-5 lines. White space is oxygen on the page. Readers need to breathe.

**Visual and kinetic:**
Only write what can be seen and heard. Not: "James thinks about his mother." Instead, show us what James does that tells us he's thinking about his mother.

**Selective:**
You're not writing the director's shot list. Don't specify camera angles (unless you're also directing). Don't describe every piece of furniture. Pick the details that matter and let the designer fill in the rest.

### The Three-Line Rule

If you can't describe what's important about a location in three lines, you're over-describing it. Establish what's essential, then get on with the scene.

### Introducing Characters

When a character first appears, give them one sentence of physical description that tells us who they are — not just what they look like.

*Generic:*
> SARAH (32) enters. She's attractive with brown hair.

*Character-revealing:*
> SARAH (32) enters still wearing her court ID badge, three coffees into a morning she thought would be short.

The second version tells us something about who she is and what kind of day she's already having.

## Parenthetical Rules

Parentheticals are the direction in parentheses after a character name: `(beat)`, `(quiet)`, `(ironic)`.

**Use them sparingly.** Every parenthetical not absolutely necessary dilutes the ones that are.

**Use them when:**
- The line is genuinely ambiguous and the wrong reading would harm the scene
- A specific physical action is inseparable from the line (she puts down her phone)
- A beat (pause) is structurally important to the scene's rhythm

**Never use them to explain the emotion:**
The dialogue should carry the emotion. If you need `(sadly)` to tell the actor how to play the line, rewrite the line.

*Bad:*
> SARAH
> (heartbroken, remembering all the good times they had)
> I guess this is goodbye.

*Better:*
> SARAH
> *(touches the door frame a moment, then lets go)*
> I guess this is goodbye.

**Common parentheticals:**

| Parenthetical | Use |
|---|---|
| `(beat)` | A brief pause. Use for rhythm. |
| `(continuing)` | After an action line interrupts a speech |
| `(into phone)` | Speaking on the phone |
| `(V.O.)` | Voiceover narration |
| `(O.S.)` | Character is off-screen but present |
| `(re: something)` | Referring to an object or person |

## Scene Checklist

Before moving to the next scene, run through this:

- [ ] Does this scene have a specific, measurable goal for the protagonist?
- [ ] Is there genuine conflict (not just absence of conflict)?
- [ ] Does the scene turn? (Yes-But or No-And)
- [ ] Does it enter as late as possible?
- [ ] Does it leave before the natural ending?
- [ ] Does something change in the relationship between characters?
- [ ] Is there at least one visual, specific detail that tells us where we are?
- [ ] Is the action in present tense with no blocks longer than 4-5 lines?
- [ ] Does any dialogue exist purely to deliver exposition? (If yes, cut or rethink it)
- [ ] If this scene were removed, would the story still work? (If yes, the scene might need to go)

## The Scene as Argument

The best dramatic scenes are arguments. Not verbal arguments (though those work too) — but competing perspectives fighting for dominance.

Every scene has a thesis (what the protagonist wants to be true) and an antithesis (what the scene forces them to confront). The outcome of the scene moves the argument forward.

The movie as a whole is an argument about the thematic question. Each scene is a sub-argument — evidence for or against the proposition the movie is making.

When you know your movie's thematic question and your scene's specific conflict, you always know what the scene is for and whether it's doing its job.
