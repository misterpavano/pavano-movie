# The DNA of the Greatest Screenplays Ever Written

This document is the result of reading, comparing, and breaking down the WGA's 101 Greatest Screenplays alongside modern classics. The goal: find the common thread. What do *Chinatown*, *Casablanca*, *Pulp Fiction*, *Parasite*, *The Silence of the Lambs*, *Good Will Hunting*, *Network*, *Groundhog Day*, and the others all share — beneath the surface, beneath genre, beneath era?

The answer isn't structure. Structure is a tool. The answer is something older.

---

## The WGA Top 10 (The Consensus Starting Point)

The Writers Guild of America polled its members in 2006. The top 10:

1. Casablanca (1942)
2. The Godfather (1972)
3. Chinatown (1974)
4. Citizen Kane (1941)
5. All About Eve (1950)
6. Annie Hall (1977)
7. Sunset Blvd. (1950)
8. Network (1976)
9. Some Like It Hot (1959)
10. The Godfather Part II (1974)

Add the modern critical consensus — *Pulp Fiction*, *Get Out*, *Parasite*, *The Silence of the Lambs*, *Good Will Hunting*, *No Country for Old Men*, *Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind*, *There Will Be Blood* — and you have your study list.

Across all of them, seven things appear without exception.

## The 7 Common Threads

---

### 1. The First Page Already Knows the Theme

Not the inciting incident. Not the protagonist's goal. The **theme**.

Read the first page of *Chinatown*: Gittes tells a grieving husband "You're right, when you're right, you're right" — while steering him out the door, away from the truth. Everything in *Chinatown* is about men being confidently, catastrophically wrong while believing they're right. Gittes will spend the entire film being absolutely certain he understands what's happening. He will be wrong until it destroys him.

Page one.

Read *Parasite*: the Kim family is crouching near the ceiling of their semi-basement apartment, hunting for a WiFi signal. They're literally living below the surface, reaching up toward something they can't access. The theme — the violence of class, the way the poor must contort themselves for scraps of the infrastructure the rich float on — is rendered in a single visual.

Read *Network*: Howard Beale tells his television audience he's going to kill himself on air. The first scene is about the moment entertainment swallowed reality. That's the whole movie.

Read *The Silence of the Lambs*: Clarice runs alone through a forest obstacle course. She clears every obstacle except one — she's too short to reach the top. She can't get there yet. She's not enough yet. But she's trying. That's her arc.

**The rule it generates:**
Your first page is not an introduction. It's a thesis statement. Not explicit — embedded in what we SEE. The audience shouldn't understand what they just saw until the film is over. Then they'll realize they knew everything from the first frame.

---

### 2. The Protagonist's External Goal Disguises Their Internal Problem

This is the deepest structural truth in screenwriting and the most commonly ignored.

In every great screenplay, there are two stories:
- The story the protagonist thinks they're in
- The story they're actually in

Gittes in *Chinatown* thinks he's in a murder mystery. He's actually in a story about what happens when a man's arrogance makes him the instrument of catastrophe.

Clarice in *Silence of the Lambs* thinks she's hunting a serial killer. She's actually in a story about what it costs a woman to become powerful in a world designed to diminish her.

Will Hunting thinks he's in a story about whether he takes a fancy job and lives up to his potential. He's actually in a story about whether a boy who was told he was worthless can believe that he deserves love.

Rick in *Casablanca* thinks he's in a story about staying neutral and surviving. He's actually in a story about whether a man who was broken by love can choose to stand for something again.

The external story gives us plot. The internal story gives us *meaning*. The genius of the great screenplays is that they make you care about the external story — the murder, the killer, the job, the war — and then reveal that the whole time, you were actually watching someone fight themselves.

**The rule it generates:**
Your protagonist needs two goals. The one they'd tell you about. And the one they're afraid to admit. The film is the gap between those two things closing.

---

### 3. The Antagonist Is the Protagonist's Shadow

Not a villain. A mirror.

The best antagonists in the history of cinema are compelling because they represent what the protagonist could have become — or what they fear they are.

Hannibal Lecter is brilliant, perceptive, and uses his intelligence as a weapon to expose and control others. Clarice is brilliant, perceptive, and uses her intelligence to protect and understand others. Same gift. Opposite application. Every scene between them is about whether Clarice can use what Lecter offers without becoming him.

In *Good Will Hunting*, Will's antagonist isn't Professor Lambeau — it's the image of himself he's constructed: worthless, violent, unlovable. The version of himself his abusers installed. The whole film is Will fighting his own internalized enemy.

In *Network*, the antagonist is the network itself — an institutional force that turns human suffering into content. Howard Beale is the protagonist. The network is what he feeds and what eventually destroys him. The system is the villain. The system has no face. That's more frightening than any individual antagonist.

Noah Cross in *Chinatown* is the most sophisticated version of this. He's Gittes without consequences. Gittes thinks he's the smartest man in the room. Cross actually is. Gittes represents the illusion of power. Cross represents real power. The confrontation between them isn't a battle between good and evil — it's a battle between who Gittes thinks he is and who men like Cross actually are.

**The rule it generates:**
Your antagonist should represent the darkest possible answer to the question your theme is asking. If your theme is "can someone choose love over safety?", your antagonist should be the person who chose safety and destroyed everything around them as a result.

### 4. Every Scene Has a Loser

Robert McKee calls this "the value shift." Every scene must move from one value state to its opposite — positive to negative, or negative to positive, or one kind of negative to a different kind of negative.

If a character enters a scene in one condition and leaves in exactly the same condition, the scene should not exist.

This is mechanical but essential. Read the opening of *Chinatown* again:
- Scene 1: Curly enters devastated, learns his wife is cheating, leaves still devastated but slightly calmer. Gittes loses money (Curly can't pay), gains a new client (Mrs. Mulwray walks in). **Value shift: financial loss, plot gain.**
- Scene 2: Mrs. Mulwray hires Gittes to follow her husband. Gittes is charmed, takes the case. **Value shift: uncertain to engaged.**
- Scene 3: Gittes observes Mulwray at the city council meeting, sees him resist the dam project. **Value shift: neutral to suspicious.**

Each scene changes the state of the world. Nothing is static. Every scene is a transaction.

In *Pulp Fiction*, this operates differently because of the non-linear structure — but watch any individual scene and a value shifts. Jules and Vincent's dialogue in the car is about their philosophical differences. By the end, Jules has shifted: the miracle in the apartment will change him. The conversation we're watching is the scene that PRECEDES that change — it establishes what's at stake when the value shift hits.

**The rule it generates:**
Before writing any scene, answer: what value is at stake? What is the state of that value when the scene begins? What is the state when it ends? If the answer is "the same," cut the scene.

---

### 5. The Dialogue Is Never About What It's About

The greatest screenwriting dialogue contains almost no direct statement of feeling. Characters don't say what they mean. They say something adjacent. The meaning lives in the gap.

The famous scene in *Good Will Hunting*: Robin Williams' character Sean keeps saying "it's not your fault" to Will. Will deflects. Jokes. Then deflects again. Then again. Then breaks. What's happening on the surface: a therapist is making a statement. What's actually happening: a man who has never been told he deserved protection is having that idea placed in front of him for the first time, and his entire defense mechanism is being dismantled.

Sean doesn't say: "You were abused and you blame yourself and I want you to stop." He says four words. Four times. The script around those four words is doing all the work.

In *Chinatown*, the theme stated moment is Gittes' line: "You can't eat the Venetian blinds, Curly. I just had 'em installed on Wednesday." This is a man who deflects emotion with practicality and wit. Every line Gittes will ever speak is compressed into this moment. He will never say "I'm afraid" or "I feel guilty." He'll redirect every emotional moment with a quip. Until he can't.

In *Casablanca*, Rick and Ilsa's reunion: "Of all the gin joints in all the towns in all the world, she walks into mine." That's not a love speech. That's a man masking devastation with performance. The performance itself is the heartbreak.

**The rule it generates:**
Write the scene your characters are trying NOT to have. Then give them dialogue for the scene they're willing to have instead. The space between those two conversations is where the great writing lives.

---

### 6. The Structure Serves the Emotional Logic, Not the Other Way Around

The greatest screenplays don't feel like they're following a structure. They feel inevitable.

*Pulp Fiction* is non-linear. It violates every conventional structural rule. But watch what Tarantino is actually doing: he's structuring emotionally, not chronologically. The film opens and closes in the diner — the robbery scene. The emotional journey between those two moments is about redemption. Jules has a moment of grace (the apartment miracle). He chooses to walk away from violence. The film ends at the same diner — now we see Jules put his faith into practice. The last scene, chronologically, is the emotional climax of a non-linear story.

*Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind* runs backward through a relationship's memory. But the structure isn't random — it's built to make you feel exactly what the characters are feeling: the loss of something precious you can only appreciate as it disappears. The non-linear structure is the theme made structural.

*Chinatown* is a classic linear detective story — except it inverts the genre. The detective in noir fiction solves the mystery and restores order. Gittes solves the mystery and the solving destroys everything. The structure is identical to noir but the emotional meaning is the opposite. Towne understood structure well enough to subvert it.

**The rule it generates:**
Don't follow structure. Understand it well enough that you can break it in service of your story's emotional logic. The three-act structure isn't a constraint — it's a description of how human attention and emotion naturally move. Work with that movement, or consciously against it. But know which you're doing.

---

### 7. The Ending Was Inevitable From the Beginning

This is the hardest one. And the most separating factor between good scripts and great ones.

When *Chinatown* ends — Evelyn dead, Cross victorious, Gittes helpless — it doesn't feel like a twist. It feels like the only way it could have ended. Every scene has been building to this. The corruption is too embedded, too powerful, too old. Gittes, for all his competence, was never going to win. The tragedy is that he couldn't see that. That we couldn't see it either, until the end, is the craft.

When *The Silence of the Lambs* ends — Clarice gets her badge, Lecter disappears into the world — the ending feels complete because both arcs are resolved. Clarice becomes Agent Starling. Lecter remains free. The world is not restored to order. But the human being at the center of it has been transformed. That's the right ending for the right story.

When *Good Will Hunting* ends — Will drives to California to find Skylar — the ending works because it's Will choosing his Need over his Want. He's been running from intimacy his whole life. The ending is him driving toward it. The final image is motion — he's in the car, not there yet. The transformation is the choice, not the arrival.

**The rule it generates:**
Plant your ending in your beginning. Not the plot ending — the emotional ending. What state of being does the protagonist need to arrive at? That destination should be implied — but hidden — in your opening image. When the audience reaches the ending, they should feel like they always knew this was coming. Even if they didn't.

## The Script-by-Script Breakdown: Opening Pages

The fastest way to understand what makes these scripts great is to read their first 10 pages.

### Chinatown (Robert Towne, 1974)
**What happens:** Gittes handles an infidelity case, then takes a new case from a woman claiming to be Evelyn Mulwray.
**What it establishes:** Gittes' arrogance, his competence, his blind spots. His world: money, class, heat, corruption. The fact that nothing is what it seems — the "Mrs. Mulwray" who hires him is not Evelyn Mulwray.
**The craft move:** The opening scene with Curly is a fake-out. It looks like setup. It's actually the theme: "You gotta be rich to kill somebody and get away with it." We think we're watching a PI comedy. We're actually watching the prologue to a tragedy.
**What every writer should steal:** The tonal whiplash — charming and funny, then suddenly dark. And the efficiency: by page 12, we know exactly who Gittes is, what drives him, and why he's going to fail.

---

### Pulp Fiction (Tarantino, 1994)
**What happens:** Two criminals in a diner debate robbing restaurants. They decide to do it. Freeze frame.
**What it establishes:** This is not going to be a conventional movie. The characters are articulate, philosophical criminals who discuss their work like office workers discuss coffee.
**The craft move:** The dictionary definition of "pulp" as the opening. Tarantino is telling you directly: this is pulp fiction. It is deliberately, proudly, unapologetically that. The self-awareness is the genre subversion.
**What every writer should steal:** The confidence of voice. Tarantino writes like he's not afraid of anything. Long dialogue. No apology for the length. If the dialogue is good enough, you don't need to cut it. Make the dialogue good enough.

---

### The Silence of the Lambs (Ted Tally, 1991)
**What happens:** Clarice runs an obstacle course. Crawford recruits her. She descends into the BSHCI to meet Lecter.
**What it establishes:** Clarice is capable but not yet arrived. Crawford (and the film) sees something in her that she's still growing into. The descent into Lecter's prison is the descent into her own unconscious — the thing she needs to confront to become who she needs to be.
**The craft move:** The Buffalo Bill investigation and the Lecter relationship run parallel and mirror each other. Both involve a monster. One monster she must catch. One monster she must use. The film is about whether she can do both without losing herself.
**What every writer should steal:** The Lecter introduction. He's standing at the front of his cell when Clarice arrives. He's been waiting. Everything we need to know about their dynamic — who has power in this relationship — is established without a word.

---

### Good Will Hunting (Damon/Affleck, 1997)
**What happens:** Will's genius is introduced (the chalkboard problem). His background is introduced (working-class Boston, criminal record). The two worlds collide (Lambeau sees Will solve the problem).
**What it establishes:** The central irony: the smartest person in this university is a janitor. He's not there yet — emotionally, relationally — but he's already there intellectually. The film is about collapsing the gap.
**The craft move:** The chalkboard scene. They could have had a character explain Will's genius through dialogue. Instead they make us watch him solve a graduate-level math problem on a hallway board overnight. We're shown. We believe.
**What every writer should steal:** "Show the gift before you show the wound." We love Will before we understand his damage. That order matters enormously. If we saw the trauma first, we'd feel sorry for him. Because we see the genius first, we root for him.

---

### Parasite (Bong Joon-ho & Han Jin-won, 2019)
**What happens:** The Kim family is introduced in their semi-basement. They find a WiFi signal. An opportunity arrives: Ki-woo can tutor the Parks' daughter.
**What it establishes:** The spatial hierarchy of the film — above ground (wealth, light, air), at ground level (the middle class, transition), below ground (the Kims, and later something darker). The film's entire architecture is vertical.
**The craft move:** The opening image — hunting for WiFi near the ceiling — tells you everything. This family has to work harder for access to the same infrastructure the Parks don't notice. That resentment, that hunger, that ingenuity under pressure: the whole movie.
**What every writer should steal:** The way theme is made spatial. The Parks' house is vertical — the yard above, the main floor, the kitchen, the basement. Every character's position in the house is their position in the social order. The writing is literally architectural.

## The One Rule That Covers Everything

Across every great screenplay, one principle holds:

**The story is never what it appears to be on the surface — and everything on the surface is chosen specifically to make you feel the thing underneath.**

The murder mystery in *Chinatown* is about unchecked power and inevitable tragedy.
The manhunt in *Silence of the Lambs* is about what it costs a woman to become capable in a world built to diminish her.
The genius janitor in *Good Will Hunting* is about whether someone can believe they deserve love after being told they don't.
The class satire in *Parasite* is about the violence required to maintain hierarchy, and the violence required to disrupt it.

Genre is the surface. Character is the mechanism. Theme is the destination.

Write the surface well enough that the audience follows it.
Write the theme truly enough that they remember it.

## Practical Takeaways for Your Script

1. **Read your first page cold.** Strip the character names. What does the page say about the world? Is your theme embedded in the first scene, visually, without dialogue?

2. **Can you name your protagonist's Two Goals?** The one they admit to, and the one they're afraid to admit. If you only have one, you have a plot. You don't yet have a story.

3. **Who is your antagonist's mirror?** What does your antagonist represent that your protagonist is fighting in themselves?

4. **Check every scene for a value shift.** Pick any scene in your draft. What's the value at stake? What's its state going in? Going out? If it doesn't shift, the scene shouldn't exist.

5. **Find the conversation your characters are avoiding.** Write them having the conversation they're willing to have instead. Use that version. The avoided conversation is the subtext the audience feels without being told.

6. **Does your ending rhyme with your beginning?** Put your opening image and your closing image side by side. Are they in conversation? Does the final image make the opening image more meaningful?

7. **Read 10 pages of *Chinatown*.** Just 10 pages. Then put it down and write your first 10 pages again.
