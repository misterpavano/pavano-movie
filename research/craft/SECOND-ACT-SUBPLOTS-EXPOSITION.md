# Second Act Problems, Subplots & Exposition

Three of the most common failure modes in otherwise solid scripts. Each has specific, fixable causes.

---

## Part 1: The Second Act Problem

Act Two is where most scripts die. It's 60 pages — half the script — and it has no natural engine driving it forward the way Act One (setup building to catalyst) and Act Three (climax building to resolution) do. The writer has to build that engine themselves.

### Why Second Acts Fall Apart

**The premise runs out of steam.**
If your core idea can only generate one kind of scene, you'll run out of ideas by page 45. A premise must be capable of continuous escalation and variation. If you can't generate 10 different scenes from your premise, your premise might not be strong enough for a feature.

**The midpoint isn't a real turn.**
The midpoint must fundamentally change the nature of the problem — not just add another obstacle. If your midpoint is "the antagonist gets stronger," that's an obstacle. If your midpoint is "the protagonist discovers the person they trusted is the antagonist," that's a turn. The difference: after a real midpoint, the story your protagonist thinks they're in changes. The problem they thought they were solving is revealed to be a different, harder problem.

**The protagonist goes passive.**
Act Two is where protagonists stop driving and start reacting. They wait for things to happen to them. They lose agency. The audience loses interest. The fix: at every scene in Act Two, ask — what is the protagonist actively doing to pursue their goal? If the answer is "surviving" or "waiting," rewrite the scene so they're making a choice that drives the story forward.

**Everything is saved for Act Three.**
Writers save their best reveals, their most emotional moments, and their biggest confrontations for the finale. Act Two becomes a holding pattern. The audience is being asked to wait 60 pages for the good stuff. Don't save anything. Front-load Act Two with consequence. The audience should be hit hard at the midpoint, again at the 65-page mark, again at All Is Lost. If the biggest emotional moment of your script is the finale, you need more emotional moments in Act Two.

---

### The Act Two Fix: Split It in Half

Think of Act Two not as one 60-page block but as two 30-page acts:

**Act 2A (pages 30-55): The Promise**
The protagonist enters the new world. They're pursuing their goal. Things are going relatively well or at least they're making progress. The Fun and Games section. The tone is: we're in this, let's see what happens.

**Act 2B (pages 55-85): The Collapse**
After the midpoint turns everything, things start going wrong. The pressure builds from every direction. The protagonist makes mistakes. The consequences stack. The tone is: everything is getting worse.

The midpoint is the hinge between 2A and 2B. Before the midpoint, the protagonist believes they can win by doing what they've been doing. After the midpoint, that belief is destroyed.

---

### The Act Two Toolkit

Five things you can add to a sagging Act Two:

**1. Raise the personal stakes.**
The external stakes are established. Now make it personal. The protagonist's relationship with someone they love is threatened. Something they believed in is revealed to be false. The cost of failure is no longer abstract.

**2. Introduce a complication from an unexpected source.**
The protagonist is making progress on the main problem. Then something unexpected — not from the main antagonist — creates a new problem. A friend becomes an obstacle. An ally turns out to have competing interests. The world doesn't cooperate.

**3. Force a moral compromise.**
The protagonist has to do something they swore they wouldn't do to stay in the game. This raises the stakes emotionally and reveals character. It also creates dramatic irony — the audience sees the protagonist becoming something they're trying to fight.

**4. Use the B Story to challenge the A Story.**
Whatever the B Story character represents (usually the theme), have them directly challenge the protagonist's approach to the A Story. The protagonist should be forced to defend their choices — and be unable to fully do so, because deep down they know the B Story character is right.

**5. The false victory/false defeat cycle.**
Act Two should feel like a wave pattern: good → bad → worse → slightly better → much worse. Not a steady decline. The moments of apparent progress make the collapses hit harder.

## Part 2: Subplots

A subplot is not a second story competing with the main story. It's a second angle on the same story.

### What Subplots Actually Do

**They carry the theme.** The main plot is often too busy with external events to pause for thematic examination. The subplot creates the space. The B Story character says the thing the protagonist can't hear yet. The subplot relationship embodies what the protagonist needs to learn.

**They reveal character dimensions the main plot can't.** The main plot puts your protagonist under one kind of pressure. The subplot puts them under a different kind. The combination gives us a three-dimensional person.

**They control pacing.** A scene that cuts away from the main tension to a subplot scene gives the audience a breath — and can be used to build dread by withholding resolution of the main plot while we watch the subplot.

**They expand the world.** A well-constructed subplot makes the film's world feel populated, real, and consequential beyond the protagonist's immediate concerns.

### The Subplot Rules

**Rule 1: Every subplot must connect to the main plot by Act Three.**
If the subplot resolves completely independently of the main plot, it's a separate short film. The connection doesn't have to be literal — it can be thematic — but the two stories must inform each other by the end.

**Rule 2: The subplot must have its own arc.**
Beginning, middle, end. Not just a running thread — a change. The subplot relationship starts somewhere and ends somewhere different.

**Rule 3: The subplot character must embody the theme.**
Not just provide contrast — embody. The subplot character's situation should be a version of the protagonist's situation, seen from a different angle, that illuminates what's really at stake in the main plot.

**Rule 4: No more than two active subplots.**
Three subplots is a different movie that doesn't know it. Two is the maximum. Usually one strong B Story is enough.

### The Bad Subplot vs. The Good Subplot

**Bad:** The protagonist has a romantic subplot that is introduced, developed, and resolved entirely independently of their main goal. The love interest appears, disappears when the main plot heats up, and reappears for the ending. The subplot is decoration.

**Good:** The protagonist's romantic relationship is in tension with their main goal. The choices they make in pursuit of their goal damage the relationship. The relationship forces them to confront their wound. The resolution of the relationship is directly connected to the resolution of the main plot — because the same internal problem is driving both.

## Part 3: Exposition

Exposition is any information the audience needs to understand the story. It's also the thing most likely to kill a script's first thirty pages.

### The Fundamental Problem

Exposition is inherently undramatic. Drama requires conflict. Exposition requires the absence of conflict — characters have to stop what they're doing to exchange information. The audience can feel when a scene exists to deliver information rather than to dramatize conflict. They call it "boring." It is boring. Because it is.

### The Six Methods (From Worst to Best)

**Method 1 (Worst): The Maid and Butler**
Two characters who already know everything tell each other things they both know, for the audience's benefit. "As you know, Bob, we've been working on this project for ten years." No one says things like this in real life. Avoid entirely.

**Method 2: The Fish Out of Water**
Put your protagonist somewhere unfamiliar so they naturally need things explained to them. It's better than the maid-and-butler because the protagonist's ignorance is motivated. But it can make the protagonist seem passive and creates its own cliché.

**Method 3: The Voice-Over**
Can work brilliantly (*Sunset Blvd.*, *Goodfellas*, *Taxi Driver*). Can be a crutch. The rule: if the information can be delivered visually or through scene, it should be. Voice-over is for information that has no visual equivalent — psychological interiority, ironic commentary, information that spans time.

**Method 4: The Document / Environmental Exposition**
Deliver information through what the protagonist reads, sees, and interacts with in the environment. News reports. Letters. Photographs. Crime scene evidence. The protagonist's bookshelf tells us who they are. Their apartment tells us their history. This is showing rather than telling — better.

**Method 5: Bury Exposition in Conflict**
Make the scene dramatic first. Let the information come out because the characters are fighting, negotiating, seducing, or deceiving each other — not because the script needs to deliver information. The audience absorbs the exposition while watching the drama. This is the correct approach for most exposition.

*Example:* Two characters arguing about whether to go back to the house. One of them, in trying to convince the other, reveals that her sister died in that house ten years ago. We learn the backstory because it matters to the argument — not because the script paused to deliver it.

**Method 6 (Best): Don't Explain It**
The audience is smart. They will fill in gaps. They don't need to know why the character is estranged from their father — they can infer from the behavior. They don't need a full backstory — a single specific detail implies the whole. Trust the audience to understand implication. Give them the detail that unlocks the inference, not the full explanation.

### The Exposition Checklist

Before every scene heavy with background information, ask:

1. **Does the audience actually need this information?** If they can follow the story without it, cut it.
2. **Do they need it NOW?** Exposition is most powerful when the audience has a question it answers. Wait until they're asking.
3. **Can it be delivered through conflict?** If yes, restructure the scene so it comes out through conflict.
4. **Can a visual deliver it instead of dialogue?** If yes, write the visual.
5. **Can a single specific detail do the work of three explanatory sentences?** Almost always yes. Find the detail.

### The "Iceberg" Principle

Hemingway's iceberg: the writer knows everything about the character and the world. The reader sees one-eighth of it. The seven-eighths beneath the surface is what gives the visible eighth its weight.

Write deep backstory for every character. Know their full history. Then put almost none of it in the script. What you know will affect every choice you make about how those characters behave. The audience will feel the depth without being told what's there.

The backstory you write for yourself is not the exposition you deliver. It's the foundation beneath the story you're telling.
