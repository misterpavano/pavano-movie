# Story Structure

The mechanics underneath every screenplay that works.

Structure isn't a formula you follow. It's the rhythm an audience expects at an unconscious level. When you violate it without purpose, they feel it as boredom or confusion, even if they can't name what's wrong. When you use it well, you're working with human psychology, not against it.

---

## Three-Act Structure

The bedrock. Everything else is built on top of this.

A 110-page screenplay breaks roughly:

- **Act One** — pages 1-25 (setup)
- **Act Two A** — pages 25-60 (escalating complications)
- **Act Two B** — pages 60-85 (protagonist at lowest point, new plan)
- **Act Three** — pages 85-110 (climax and resolution)

### The Exact Beat Map

**Pages 1-10: The World Before**
Introduce your protagonist in their ordinary world. Show us who they are, what they want, what's missing, and what their flaw is — all through action and behavior, never exposition. The audience needs someone to follow. Give them reasons to care.

_The mistake writers make here:_ Over-explaining. If you're writing dialogue that establishes backstory in the first five pages, you've already lost. Show us behavior. Characters reveal themselves by what they do, not what they say about themselves.

**Page 10-12: Catalyst / Inciting Incident**
Something happens that disrupts the ordinary world. This is NOT the "real" story yet — it's the knock on the door. It forces a choice.

_Examples:_
- A murder is discovered (*Chinatown*: the woman in Gittes' office)
- An invitation arrives (*The Wizard of Oz*: the tornado)
- A diagnosis is delivered (*50/50*: Joseph finds out he has cancer)

**Pages 12-25: Debate**
The protagonist resists the call. They weigh their options. This section humanizes your protagonist — they're not a superhero who immediately charges in. They're a person with something to lose. The Debate ends when they commit to crossing the threshold.

**Page 25: Break into Act Two — The Lock-In**
The protagonist makes a choice that commits them to the story's world. There is no going back. This is sometimes called the "first turning point" or the "lock-in." After this, the protagonist is in the new world (physically or psychologically).

_The test:_ If your protagonist can just walk away after page 25, you haven't locked them in hard enough.

**Pages 25-50: Fun and Games / B-Story Emerges**
The protagonist explores the new world. This is often the most entertaining section — the promise of the premise being delivered. If your logline is "what if a hitman had to go to high school," pages 25-50 are where you pay that off.

The B-Story (usually a love story or mentor relationship) enters here and carries the theme.

**Page 50: Midpoint**
A false victory or false defeat. The protagonist appears to achieve their external goal, or loses spectacularly. Stakes double. The fun-and-games section ends. The real battle begins.

After the Midpoint, the protagonist stops reacting and starts acting. They make a plan. They take the offensive. This is the crucial structural shift.

**Pages 50-75: Bad Guys Close In**
The opposition escalates. The protagonist's flaws are exposed under pressure. Everything they've built starts to crack. Allies betray them or fail them. The external threat intensifies.

**Pages 75-80: All Is Lost / Dark Night of the Soul**
The lowest point. The protagonist has failed. They've lost what they wanted AND what they needed. Death (literal or metaphorical) hovers over the scene.

_The "whiff of death" rule:_ Something has to die here — a relationship, a belief system, a literal life. If nothing dies, the All Is Lost doesn't land.

This beat is where most amateur scripts go soft. Writers protect their protagonist. Don't. Destroy them here.

**Pages 80-85: Dark Night of the Soul**
The protagonist sits with their failure. This is the beat writers skip most often and shouldn't. Let the protagonist feel the loss. This pause creates emotional depth and sets up the breakthrough.

**Page 85: Break into Act Three — The Synthesis**
Something clicks. The protagonist finds their answer — usually by combining the lesson of the B-Story with the tools of Act One. They create a new plan, born from who they've become, not who they were.

**Pages 85-110: Act Three — Climax and Resolution**
The protagonist executes their new plan. The climax confrontation occurs (protagonist vs. antagonist, protagonist vs. self). The thematic question is answered definitively. Loose ends resolve.

_The rule:_ The protagonist must be active in solving the climax. If someone rescues them, you've written the wrong ending.

## Save the Cat — The 15 Beats

Blake Snyder's beat sheet is the most widely used structural tool in Hollywood development. Every major studio executive knows it. Most working screenwriters have internalized it.

The name comes from the first beat: do something in the first ten pages that makes the audience fall in love with your protagonist. (Classic example: they save a cat. They do something kind, selfless, or vulnerable.)

Here are all 15 beats with page targets for a 110-page screenplay:

| Beat | Page | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Opening Image | 1 | Snapshot of the protagonist's world before. Should mirror/contrast the Final Image. |
| Theme Stated | 5 | Someone states the movie's theme, often to the protagonist, who dismisses it. |
| Set-Up | 1-10 | Introduce the world, the protagonist, their flaw, their want, their need. Plant everything you'll pay off. |
| Catalyst | 12 | The event that changes everything. The story arrives. |
| Debate | 12-25 | Should I engage? Can I do this? What do I have to lose? |
| Break into Two | 25 | The protagonist commits. The new world begins. |
| B-Story | 30 | The love story or relationship that carries the theme enters. |
| Fun and Games | 30-55 | The promise of the premise delivered. The reason people bought a ticket. |
| Midpoint | 55 | False victory or false defeat. Stakes double. Protagonist shifts from reactive to active. |
| Bad Guys Close In | 55-75 | External and internal pressure mounts. Allies fail. Flaws exposed. |
| All Is Lost | 75 | Worst moment. Something dies. |
| Dark Night of the Soul | 75-85 | Protagonist at rock bottom, processing failure. |
| Break into Three | 85 | The a-ha moment. New plan, born from synthesis. |
| Finale | 85-110 | Protagonist executes new plan, faces antagonist, theme answered. |
| Final Image | 110 | Mirror of Opening Image — shows us how the world (and protagonist) has changed. |

### The Opening Image / Final Image Mirror

This is Snyder's most elegant structural demand. The Opening Image and Final Image should be visual bookends that show transformation.

- *Rocky*: Opens with Rocky losing a club fight in an empty gym. Closes with Rocky surrounded by love, having gone the distance.
- *Legally Blonde*: Opens with Elle in a pink bubble, expecting a proposal. Closes with Elle at Harvard graduation, having earned real respect.
- *Silver Linings Playbook*: Opens with Pat in a mental institution. Closes with Pat dancing, in love, integrated back into life.

The transformation shown in your Final Image answers: **what changed because this story happened?**

### Theme Stated

Most writers forget this beat or make it too subtle. At around page 5, someone says the theme out loud — usually to the protagonist, who rejects it or misunderstands it. They'll understand it by the end.

In *The Dark Knight*: "You either die a hero or you live long enough to see yourself become the villain." Batman dismisses this. By the end, he chooses to live as a villain in the public eye to preserve the city's hope.

## The Hero's Journey (12 Stages)

Joseph Campbell's Monomyth, adapted by Christopher Vogler in *The Writer's Journey*. Useful less as a rigid beat sheet and more as a map of psychological transformation.

### Stage 1: Ordinary World
The hero exists in a limited, ordinary world before the adventure. This establishes their normal — the comfort zone they'll be forced to leave. The contrast makes the Special World feel genuinely extraordinary.

### Stage 2: Call to Adventure
The hero is presented with a problem, challenge, or adventure. Something disrupts the ordinary world. This can be external (a threat arrives) or internal (a desire awakens).

### Stage 3: Refusal of the Call
The hero hesitates, refuses, or feels fear. This is essential — it makes the hero human. If they leap immediately toward adventure, we don't believe them as a real person.

### Stage 4: Meeting the Mentor
The hero meets a mentor figure who gives them training, knowledge, magical equipment, or wisdom that prepares them for the journey ahead. The mentor can't go on the journey for them — they just prepare them.

_Examples:_ Morpheus (The Matrix), Obi-Wan (Star Wars), Haymitch (Hunger Games)

### Stage 5: Crossing the First Threshold
The hero commits to the adventure, crossing into the Special World. This is the point of no return — Act One ends here. Often guarded by a Threshold Guardian who tests their commitment.

### Stage 6: Tests, Allies, and Enemies
The hero learns the rules of the Special World through a series of tests. They make allies and enemies. They fail small and learn.

This is Act Two A — the hero is still reactive, still learning.

### Stage 7: Approach to the Inmost Cave
The hero and allies prepare for the major challenge. Plans are made, reconnaissance done. Emotions run high. This is the calm before the storm.

### Stage 8: The Ordeal
The hero faces the supreme ordeal — their greatest fear, a life-or-death crisis, a death-and-rebirth. This is the Midpoint and/or All Is Lost beat. The hero must die (metaphorically or literally) and be reborn.

### Stage 9: Reward (Seizing the Sword)
Having survived the ordeal, the hero claims the reward — an object, knowledge, reconciliation, a physical object. They haven't won the war yet. This is the calm after the storm, briefly.

### Stage 10: The Road Back
The hero must return to the Ordinary World with the reward. The antagonist often pursues. There's a choice: do they really want to return? The shadow world isn't finished with them.

### Stage 11: Resurrection
The hero faces a final, climactic crisis — one last test in which they must use everything they've learned. They're refined by fire. Old self must truly die so the new self can live. This is the final confrontation.

### Stage 12: Return with the Elixir
The hero returns to the Ordinary World with treasure — physical or spiritual. They bring something that heals the ordinary world, whether that's an object, a lesson, or their own changed self.

## The Sequence Method

Less well-known than Save the Cat but beloved by film school writers. Eight 12-15 page sequences, each with its own tension and mini-resolution. Developed by Frank Daniel at the AFI.

The power of the Sequence Method: each sequence is like a short film, with its own inciting incident, rising action, and resolution. The resolution of each sequence sets up the question of the next.

**Sequence 1 (pp. 1-15):** Establish ordinary world and introduce the story question. Ends with the protagonist facing the initial disruption.

**Sequence 2 (pp. 15-30):** Protagonist decides to engage. First threshold crossed. Ends with protagonist committed to the world of the story.

**Sequence 3 (pp. 30-45):** Protagonist attempts first approach to main goal. Early success creates overconfidence. Ends with the first major setback.

**Sequence 4 (pp. 45-60):** Protagonist recovers, recalibrates, finds new approach. Midpoint false victory/defeat. Ends with the real stakes becoming clear.

**Sequence 5 (pp. 60-75):** Complications multiply. Protagonist takes bigger risks. Relationships under strain. Ends with protagonist's plan in ruins.

**Sequence 6 (pp. 75-90):** All Is Lost. Dark Night of the Soul. Protagonist faces total failure. Ends with the breakthrough — new understanding that changes everything.

**Sequence 7 (pp. 90-100):** Protagonist executes the new plan. Final confrontation approaches. Ends with the point of no return before the climax.

**Sequence 8 (pp. 100-110):** Climax and resolution. Thematic question answered. Final image. Ends.

## Blake Snyder's Genre Categories

Snyder identified 10 genre categories. Understanding which category your script falls into tells you what the genre's rules are — and which beats the audience is expecting.

**Monster in the House**
A monster (literal or figurative) is trapped in a confined space with the hero. The sin that unleashed the monster must be atoned.
_Examples:_ Jaws, Halloween, Alien, Panic Room

**Golden Fleece**
The hero goes on a road trip (physical or metaphorical) in pursuit of something, and the journey transforms them. The prize is almost never the point.
_Examples:_ Wizard of Oz, Star Wars, Legally Blonde, Road Trip

**Out of the Bottle**
A wish is granted, and the hero must learn from the consequences. Usually a wish-fulfillment with a reversal — the thing you wanted turns out to be what you shouldn't have asked for.
_Examples:_ Liar Liar, Bruce Almighty, Freaky Friday, Groundhog Day

**Dude with a Problem**
An ordinary person in an extraordinary situation. The hero has done nothing to deserve their predicament — they're just in it.
_Examples:_ Die Hard, Schindler's List, Gravity, North by Northwest

**Rites of Passage**
Life transitions that everyone faces (adolescence, death, addiction, divorce). The pain of change, and the acceptance of a new world.
_Examples:_ About Schmidt, Ordinary People, Requiem for a Dream, 10

**Buddy Love**
Two incomplete people who clash, find each other's flaws insufferable, and ultimately heal each other.
_Examples:_ Lethal Weapon, Rain Man, The Proposal, When Harry Met Sally

**Whydunit**
The detective story. The hero uncovers a mystery that reveals uncomfortable truths about the world and themselves.
_Examples:_ Chinatown, All the President's Men, Se7en, Zodiac

**Fool Triumphant**
The underdog, the outsider, the innocent who succeeds where everyone said they couldn't. The fool reveals the hypocrisy of the establishment.
_Examples:_ Being There, Forrest Gump, Shakespeare in Love, Billy Elliot

**Institutionalized**
A group dynamic — joining, transforming, leaving, or destroying an institution. The tension between individual identity and group conformity.
_Examples:_ MASH, One Flew Over the Cuckoo's Nest, Full Metal Jacket, The Godfather

**Superhero**
A special being (with special powers or a special code) must learn to use those powers for the right reasons. Usually involves a nemesis who is a dark mirror.
_Examples:_ Iron Man, Batman Begins, The Matrix, Edward Scissorhands

## Logline Formula

**Structure:**
> When [protagonist with defining flaw/want] [inciting incident/catalyst], they must [stakes-laden goal] before [ticking clock/consequence] — or risk [ultimate cost].

The key elements:
1. **Protagonist** — described by their flaw OR their goal, not their job
2. **Inciting Incident** — specific, active, visual
3. **Goal** — clear external objective
4. **Stakes** — what happens if they fail
5. **Irony** — the best loglines have inherent irony that creates tension just from the setup

### 10 Famous Loglines Broken Down

**Jaws:**
*When a great white shark terrorizes a small beach town, the police chief (who fears the water) must overcome his phobia and kill the shark before it destroys the town's economy and his credibility.*
→ Irony: the cop who fears water has to go into the water to save the town.

**The Silence of the Lambs:**
*An FBI trainee must win the trust of an imprisoned cannibalistic genius to catch a serial killer who's already targeting his next victim.*
→ Irony: you need the monster to catch the monster.

**Toy Story:**
*When a new toy threatens his place as a child's favorite, a pull-string cowboy must work with his rival to get home before they're left behind forever.*
→ Irony: enemies must cooperate.

**Get Out:**
*When a young Black man visits his white girlfriend's parents, he discovers their idyllic liberal family is hiding a horrific secret.*
→ The racial anxiety subtext is baked into the premise.

**Groundhog Day:**
*A cynical weatherman finds himself reliving the same day over and over until he becomes a genuinely better person.*
→ The premise IS the character arc. The structure forces the theme.

**Parasite:**
*A poor family worms its way into a wealthy household — only to discover another family with the same idea.*
→ The reversal is hidden in the setup. The second-half bomb is set up structurally from the start.

**Whiplash:**
*A young jazz drummer must survive the sadistic training of the best music teacher in the country — without losing his humanity in the pursuit of greatness.*
→ The antagonist is also the protagonist's greatest opportunity. Classic ambivalence.

**The Social Network:**
*The founder of Facebook — driven by a need to prove himself after being rejected — builds the world's largest social network while systematically betraying every friend he has.*
→ The irony: the guy who built the world's biggest social network has no friends.

**Tootsie:**
*A difficult, unemployable actor disguises himself as a woman to get a soap opera role — and becomes a better man for it.*
→ The costume forces the character arc.

**Hereditary:**
*After her mother dies, a woman begins uncovering dark secrets about her family's history — while a terrible evil begins dismantling her family.*
→ Grief as the entry point for a deeper horror. The premise earns the emotional depth.
