# Adaptation, IP, and Rights

Adaptation is one of the most commercially viable paths in Hollywood. Studios prefer known IP — it reduces marketing risk. But adapting existing material comes with legal landmines that will kill your project if you don't navigate them correctly.

---

## Types of Adaptation

**Book to Film**
The most common. A novel, memoir, or non-fiction book becomes a feature or series.

**True Story / Real Events**
A documented event — crime, scandal, historical moment — becomes a script. May or may not require rights depending on what sources you use.

**Life Rights**
A living person's story. Requires their explicit consent unless you're only using information in the public record.

**Short Story, Article, or Podcast**
Source material from journalism, long-form magazine pieces, or podcasts. The New Yorker, The Atlantic, Serial — all have been optioned.

**Existing IP (Video Games, Comics, Other Films)**
Requires studio/IP holder licensing. Not a realistic path for an emerging writer without backing.

---

## The Option Agreement: How It Works

You can't just write an adaptation and sell it. You need to control the rights first — or have a producer agree to acquire them contingent on your script selling.

**The option:** You pay the rights holder for the *exclusive right* to purchase the full rights within a specified window — typically 12-18 months, sometimes with a renewal option for another 12-18 months.

**Option fee:** Negotiated. For an unknown writer optioning an unknown book: $1 (yes, one dollar) to $5,000 is common. The option fee is usually "against" the purchase price — meaning when you exercise the option and buy the rights outright, the option fee counts toward the full purchase price.

**Purchase price:** What you pay when you actually sell the underlying rights. For a published book with moderate readership: $25,000-$100,000 is a common range. A bestseller with cultural relevance: significantly more.

**What the option gives you:** Exclusivity. No one else can adapt this material while your option is active. You can develop the script, pitch it, and try to sell it — with the rights attached.

**The typical sequence:**
1. You fall in love with a book
2. You contact the author or their literary agent
3. You negotiate an option agreement (a lawyer should review this)
4. You write the script under option
5. If a producer or studio wants to buy it, they acquire the rights from the author (using your option) and hire you to continue developing it — or buy you out

**Critical:** If your option expires while your script is unsold, you lose the rights. The author can option to someone else. Get a renewal clause in your agreement.

## True Stories and Life Rights

### The Facts Are Free. The Expression Isn't.

Facts themselves are not copyrightable. The fact that a specific murder happened in 1987 is in the public record. Anyone can write a screenplay about it. What's protected is the specific creative expression of those facts — the narrative structure, characterization, and writing of a book about that murder.

**The practical rule:** If you're using a published book as your primary source, you need the rights to that book. If you're constructing your story from independent research — court documents, newspaper articles, interviews, public records — you likely don't need rights to any single source.

**Document your research.** If you're writing a true story from public sources, keep records of every source you used. If it ever comes to a legal challenge, proof that you assembled your story independently (not copied from a single work) is your defense.

### Life Story Rights

If your story features a living person, you need to think carefully about life rights.

**Facts about public figures** in their public capacity are freely usable. A senator's voting record, a CEO's public statements, a criminal's documented crimes — these are public record.

**What's not free:** portraying a real living person committing crimes they didn't commit, in a false sexual context, in a way that would be defamatory, or using their specific likeness in a way that implies endorsement.

**Why you should get life rights anyway:**
- The person may cooperate and provide information you couldn't get otherwise
- It protects you from defamation and invasion of privacy claims
- Studios will often require life rights before greenlighting production
- It gives you exclusivity — no one else can do the "authorized version"

**Compensation for life rights:** Highly variable. A private individual telling an extraordinary story might accept a modest option fee ($5,000-$25,000). A public figure who has been courted by multiple studios might command six figures.

## The Adaptation Craft: What Changes When You Adapt

Adaptation is not transcription. A book and a film are different forms with different capabilities. The greatest adaptations preserve the soul of the source material while making ruthless structural decisions.

### What Books Have That Films Don't

**Interior monologue.** A novel can spend 10 pages in a character's head. A film has to externalize everything. Every thought, feeling, and realization must become behavior, dialogue, or visual.

**Time.** A novel can cover 40 years in 300 pages. A film has 90-120 minutes. You will cut — the question is what.

**Density.** A 400-page novel contains enough material for a 5-hour film. You are making a 100-minute film. Something has to go.

### The Three Adaptation Questions

**1. What is the soul of this story?**
Not the plot — the emotional core. The thing that made the book resonate. If you preserve the plot and lose the soul, you've made a bad adaptation. If you change the plot and preserve the soul, you've made a great one.

**2. What must be cut?**
Subplots, secondary characters, backstory, description. Everything that can't be filmed or doesn't serve the film's central question.

**3. What must be invented?**
Books don't have scenes — they have narrative. You will invent dialogue that was never in the book. You will invent scenes that externalize what was interior. You will invent moments that compress what the book extended over chapters. This is not unfaithfulness — it's the craft of the form.

### The Compression Problem

The most common adaptation failure: keeping too much. The writer is faithful to the source material to the point of incoherence. Every subplot is preserved. Every character is present. The film is two hours and forty minutes and the audience feels all of them.

The fix: identify your film's central question. One question. Keep everything that serves it. Cut everything that doesn't.

*Schindler's List* (from Thomas Keneally's novel) is about whether one man's moral awakening can mean anything in the face of systematic evil. Everything in the film serves that question. The novel contains much more — Zaillian's screenplay stripped it to the essential.

*No Country for Old Men* (from Cormac McCarthy's novel) preserves the novel's moral argument almost intact while cutting the interior monologue. The Coen Brothers solved the interiority problem by giving it to Sheriff Bell — making his POV the film's frame, letting the film's events be filtered through his failing understanding.

## 2024-2025 Black List: What's Getting Attention

The Black List's 2024 and 2025 annual lists (the most-liked unproduced specs as voted by development executives) show clear patterns:

**What's landing:**
- Psychological thrillers with a high-concept premise (a nurse caring for a murder suspect who may be faking Alzheimer's; a woman investigating her own mysterious abduction)
- Dark satire with sharp social commentary (parents manufacturing trauma for college admissions; a night when a specific societal rule is lifted)
- Character studies under extreme moral pressure (a former rodeo star pulled back into crime seeking redemption; a disillusioned teacher leading students into crime)
- Contained thrillers where the premise is the hook (suburban couple invites a stranger in with deadly results)
- Genre-blending that doesn't feel like genre-blending — the horror or thriller is in service of a real emotional story

**What's NOT landing:**
- AI-themed scripts (2025 Black List had notably fewer than 2024 — the fascination has peaked, the market is cautious)
- Pure genre without a fresh angle
- Straight biographical scripts without a strong structural hook

**The common thread in both years:**
The best specs on the Black List all have a premise that is immediately understandable, immediately generates tension, and immediately raises a question that only watching the film can answer. The logline IS the movie. The script delivers on the logline.

## AI and the WGA: What You Need to Know

The 2023 WGA MBA (running through May 2026) established the following rules that matter to you:

**AI cannot be a "writer."** AI-generated material is not "literary material" under the WGA agreement. It cannot receive writing credit.

**Companies cannot require you to use AI.** If you're hired under a WGA agreement, they cannot force you to use generative AI to produce your work.

**You CAN use AI with company consent.** Using AI as a tool — for brainstorming, research, or developmental work — is permitted when the company agrees.

**AI output cannot be used to reduce your credit or compensation.** If a studio gives you AI-generated material to work from, that material doesn't count as "source material" that reduces your compensation. You're treated as the first writer.

**The copyright reality:** Pure AI-generated text is not copyrightable under current U.S. law. Human editing and creative direction of AI output IS copyrightable. The practical rule: your screenplay must be substantively yours. If AI wrote significant portions of your script, you may not be able to copyright it — and you certainly can't register it with the WGA as your work.

**For our purposes (using AI to assist development):**
Using AI for research, to analyze structure, to pressure-test loglines, to generate beat sheet options you then evaluate — all of this is tool use. The creative decisions remain yours. The writing remains yours. That's the correct relationship with AI in the screenwriting process.

The script in this repo — if we write it — will be ours. The research and development tools are assistance. The creative authorship is human.
