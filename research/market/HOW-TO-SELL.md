# How to Sell a Screenplay

The business side. Most writers avoid this. That's why most writers don't sell.

---

## The Two Markets: Spec vs. Assignment

**Spec screenplay:** You write it on your own time, with your own money, and try to sell it. This is how most writers start. High risk, high potential reward.

**Assignment/WGA work:** A production company or studio hires you to write something. You write what they want, for a negotiated fee. This is how professional screenwriters make their living.

The path: write a great spec → get representation → use the spec to demonstrate your writing ability → get assignments.

Almost nobody sells their first spec. The first spec (and the second, and the third) gets you representation. The fourth or fifth one might sell. The assignments that come after representation are where the income actually is.

This is not pessimism. It's career math. Know it going in.

## The Pitch Documents

Before you can sell anything, you need the documents that get you in the room.

### The Logline

One sentence. The whole movie, compressed. Your single most important marketing document.

The logline doesn't need to explain every plot detail — it needs to create curiosity and demonstrate the premise's commercial potential.

**Formula:** When [protagonist with flaw/want] [inciting incident], they must [goal with stakes] before [consequence].

A good logline should make a busy executive say "I want to read that" or "I could see that movie." If it doesn't do that, rewrite it.

See `pitch/templates/LOGLINE-TEMPLATE.md` for 20 breakdowns.

### The One-Pager / Brief Treatment

A one-page document covering:
- Title, genre, format (feature vs. limited series)
- Logline
- 3-4 paragraph synopsis (beginning, middle, end — no cliffhangers; tell the ending)
- Comps (2 films that capture the tone and commercial space)
- Why now / the hook

Used when: querying production companies and managers who want more than a logline but less than a full treatment.

### The Treatment

5-15 pages. A full narrative summary of the screenplay, written in present tense, cinematic prose. Tells the whole story with character and theme present.

Used when: a production company wants to read more before committing to the full script, or when you're being hired to develop an original idea.

Treatments are hard to write well. Most are too long, too dry, or too plot-heavy. A great treatment reads like a short story — not like a Wikipedia summary.

### The Pitch Deck

For more visual presentations (limited series, high-concept features), a visual document with:
- Title/tagline
- Logline
- Series bible (for TV)
- Character breakdowns
- Visual tone references
- Episode summaries (for TV)

Used when: pitching to streaming development teams or packaged projects.

## Managers vs. Agents

Both represent writers. They have different roles.

### Literary Managers
- Develop your career long-term
- Provide notes and help shape your work
- Make introductions to producers and production companies
- Take 10-15% commission
- **Regulated by the Talent Agencies Act in California (but less strictly than agents)**
- Can produce the work (agents legally cannot)

**For unknown writers:** Literary managers are your first target. They take on newer writers. They're actively looking for undiscovered talent to build careers with. This is where you start.

### Literary Agents
- Negotiate and close deals
- Submit scripts to studios and networks
- Have direct relationships with buyers (not just developers)
- Take 10% commission
- **Regulated by the Talent Agencies Act — they cannot produce the work**
- Generally require you to already have traction (representation is easier once a manager is involved)

**The pipeline:** Manager finds you → helps develop and position your work → introduces you to agents when you have enough traction → agent submits and closes deals.

Some writers start with an agent. Most start with a manager.

## Cold Querying: The System

This is how you get a manager when you have no connections.

### Step 1: Build the List

Research literary managers who represent screenplay writers. Sources:
- IMDbPro (search films you love, find the writers, find their representatives)
- Query Tracker (screenwriting section)
- Done Deal Pro (for deal news and rep information)
- Stage 32 (connects writers with reps and producers)
- The Black List (curated script marketplace)

Build a list of 30-50 managers. Note which ones rep writers of material similar to yours.

### Step 2: Write the Query Letter

Short. One page. Three paragraphs maximum.

**Paragraph 1:** The hook. Your logline + what makes you / this project different. Be specific. Avoid adjectives like "riveting" and "gripping."

**Paragraph 2:** Brief synopsis — 3-5 sentences. Tell the story. Include the ending. Never say "I don't want to spoil it." They're not reading it for pleasure.

**Paragraph 3:** Credentials. If you have none: skip this or say "This is my debut screenplay." Do NOT apologize for not having credits. Managers sign debut writers constantly. Do NOT list your day job unless it's deeply relevant to the script.

Closing: "The full script is available upon request. Thank you for your time."

### Step 3: Send

Email subject line format: **QUERY: [Genre] — [Title] — [Logline]**

Send 5-10 queries per week. Track every one in your submissions tracker. Do not follow up within 6 weeks.

### Response Rate Reality

Most cold queries get no response. That's normal. An 8-10% response rate (read request) is good. A 2-3% sign-rate from read requests is good. The numbers are brutal, but they're consistent — your job is to maximize surface area.

## Cold Query Template

```
Subject: QUERY: [Genre] — [TITLE]

[Manager First Name],

[TITLE] is a [Genre] feature. [1-2 sentence logline.]

[3-4 sentences of story. Beginning, middle, end. Name the protagonist, the conflict, the resolution. No cliffhangers.]

[Credentials if any. If none: "This is my first produced screenplay." OR skip entirely if you have nothing relevant.]

The full screenplay is available upon request. Thank you for your time.

[Your Name]
[Your Email]
[Your Phone — optional]
```

## Production Companies With Open Submissions

Some production companies accept unsolicited material directly (rare and valuable). Most require a query first, but some have formal submission portals.

The "open" status changes — always verify before submitting. See `WHO-TO-PITCH.md` for current list.

**General rule:** Even "open" submission policies often mean "submit a query/synopsis first." Only submit a full script when requested.

## How Optioning Works

An option is a contract in which a production company pays you for the right to try to develop your script into a produced film, for a defined period (usually 12-18 months, often with a renewal option).

**Option fee:** Typically 2.5-10% of the purchase price. For an independent producer, this might be $1,000-$10,000. For a major studio, this might be $50,000-$150,000. Some producers offer $1 options — these can be worth taking if the producer is genuinely connected and motivated.

**Purchase price:** The amount you'd receive if the film actually gets made. For a WGA minimum project, the purchase price is negotiated up front as part of the option agreement. WGA minimums for original spec screenplays are publicly available on the WGA website.

**What the option gives them:** The exclusive right to develop and produce your script during the option period. They can't produce it without paying the purchase price. You can't sell it to anyone else during that period.

**When it expires:** If the film hasn't been produced by the end of the option period, the rights revert to you. You keep the option fee. You can re-option it, keep shopping it, or just wait.

**The producer's perspective:** Options are how producers build their development slate. Most optioned projects never get made. They're bets on potential.

## WGA Registration

Register your scripts with the WGA (Writers Guild of America) before submitting them anywhere.

**WGA Registry:** wga.org/the-guild/advocacy/registration — $22 for WGA non-members. Valid for 5 years.

This creates a timestamped record of your work and establishes chain of title. It does NOT constitute copyright (register with the US Copyright Office for full protection), but it's faster and cheap.

Also register with the US Copyright Office (copyright.gov) for full legal protection. $65 online filing. Takes weeks.

Do both. The cost is trivial. The protection is real.

## The Pitch Meeting

If your query generates interest and a manager or producer wants to meet, this is what happens.

**The general meeting:** A first meeting where they get to know you and vice versa. No specific project is usually pitched. They want to know what you're working on, what you're interested in, and whether you're someone they want to work with.

Come with:
- 3-5 pitches for projects you're actively developing
- Genuine knowledge of their company/taste (read their IMDbPro page before the meeting)
- Your own questions — this is also an interview of them
- Energy. Confidence. Enthusiasm that isn't desperation.

**The pitch:** If they're interested in a specific project, you may be asked to pitch it in detail. Practice pitching your logline and 5-minute version aloud, many times, before any meeting.

**The follow-up:** If a general meeting ends with "send us your script," they're interested. If it ends with "let's stay in touch," they're politely passing. The difference is usually obvious.

**The test:**  Would you want to work with this person for 2-3 years while the project develops? Do they understand what makes your script special? Are they asking smart questions? A bad manager is worse than no manager.

## The Hollywood Query Letter — Full Example

```
Subject: QUERY: Psychological Thriller — THE COLD LEDGER

Hi Sarah,

THE COLD LEDGER is a psychological thriller in the vein of Knives Out meets 
The Gift. Here's the logline: When a forensic accountant discovers evidence of 
fraud inside her own firm, she must expose the conspiracy before the partners 
she's trusted for a decade silence her permanently.

The story: Maya Chen has spent twelve years building a reputation at Garrison 
Financial as the cleanest auditor in the business. When a routine audit reveals 
a systematic fraud scheme that's buried a $200M client loss, Maya assumes it's 
the work of a rogue employee — until she discovers the cover-up goes all the 
way to the founding partners. Isolated, discredited, and with her career being 
methodically dismantled, Maya realizes she's not just uncovering a crime: she's 
already been framed for it. As the net tightens, Maya must use the same skills 
her enemies used against her to dismantle the lie they've built — before the 
SEC investigation she triggered leads back to her.

The full screenplay is available upon request. Thank you for your time.

[Name]
[Email]
[Phone]
```

This query letter works because:
- Subject line names genre immediately
- Comps establish tone and commercial space without being defensive
- Logline is specific (forensic accountant, fraud, inside her own firm)
- Synopsis names stakes clearly and ends with the dramatic problem
- No excess adjectives, no apology for having no credits, no backstory about why this story matters to you personally
