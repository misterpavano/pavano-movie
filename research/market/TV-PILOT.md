# Writing the TV Pilot: Features vs. Pilots, Streaming vs. Network

The spec pilot is increasingly the calling card of choice for emerging writers. Features are still the primary spec market, but a strong original pilot demonstrates that you can build a world that sustains — which is what all of television needs.

---

## The Core Difference: Feature vs. Pilot

A feature film tells a complete story. By the last page, the central dramatic question is answered, the protagonist's arc is resolved, and the world is changed.

A TV pilot does the opposite. It opens a world, establishes a dramatic engine, and ends with a question that demands the next episode. By the last page, the audience should want more — not feel satisfied.

**The series engine is everything.**

A series engine is the core concept that generates infinite conflict, week after week, without being exhausted. It's the answer to: "Why does this story never end?"

*Breaking Bad*'s series engine: a terminally ill chemistry teacher becomes a drug manufacturer. The engine generates conflict from every direction — family, law enforcement, the criminal world, his own disintegrating ethics — indefinitely.

*Succession*'s engine: an aging media mogul won't name a successor. Every episode, the children try to position themselves. The engine never resolves because Logan never resolves it. The conflict is structural, not episodic.

*The Bear*'s engine: a fine-dining chef inherits a chaotic Chicago sandwich shop. The engine is the friction between the protagonist's ambition and the reality of his circumstances — compounded by grief, family, and the relentless pressure of food service.

**If your pilot concept can't sustain a series engine, it's a feature.**

Ask: after the first season, what's the second season about? If you can't answer immediately, the engine might not be there.

---

## What a Pilot Needs to Do in Its First 10 Pages

Unlike a feature — where you have until page 12 for the inciting incident — a pilot needs to establish its world, its protagonist, and its engine within the first 10 pages. Network pilots need this done by page 5.

Specifically, the first 10 pages must answer:
- What world is this? (Time, place, rules, tone)
- Who is the protagonist and what's broken about their life right now?
- What is the central dramatic tension that will drive this series?
- Why does this world generate infinite conflict?

The *pilot hook* — the moment at the end of the episode that makes the audience demand episode two — must be earned by everything that came before it.

---

## Pilot Structure: One-Hour Drama

**Teaser (pages 1-5)**
The cold open. Establishes tone, world, and a hook before the title card. Often the most cinematically ambitious sequence in the pilot. Sets the promise of the show.

**Act One (pages 5-20)**
Protagonist in their world. Status quo established. The central character dynamic introduced. Inciting incident by end of act.

**Act Two (pages 20-35)**
Protagonist engages with the disruption. New characters enter. The world expands. Complications.

**Act Three (pages 35-50)**
Stakes escalate. The core dramatic problem of the series crystallizes. The protagonist is forced to make a choice that defines who they are in this world.

**Act Four / Tag (pages 50-60)**
The pilot's conclusion — which is not a resolution. The episode's immediate conflict may be addressed. The series' central tension is deepened, not answered. The hook.

---

## Pilot Structure: Half-Hour (Comedy or Dramedy)

**Pages 1-5:** Cold open / teaser — establish world and protagonist, comedic or dramatic hook

**Pages 5-15:** Act One — protagonist's normal world, the disruption

**Pages 15-25:** Act Two — protagonist's response to disruption, complications, the core relationship dynamic established

**Pages 25-32:** Act Three — resolution of episode conflict, series engine revved up for next episode

## Streaming vs. Network: What's Different

### Network (ABC, NBC, CBS, Fox)
- Act breaks matter — commercial breaks require natural pausing points. Label them.
- Content restrictions apply — language, violence, sexuality are regulated
- Pilot season is January-April; pilots shot in spring; series ordered in May
- One-hour dramas: 42-45 minutes runtime (60-65 pages). Half-hours: 22 minutes (25-30 pages)
- Safer concepts preferred — mass-audience appeal
- Procedural formats (crime, medical, legal) have more consistent market

### Streaming (Netflix, HBO Max, Apple TV+, Amazon, Hulu)
- No act breaks required (no commercials)
- More creative freedom — mature content, unconventional structure, riskier premises
- Traditional pilot season is dissolving — streamers order year-round
- Increasing trend toward straight-to-series orders: many shows skip the pilot phase entirely and go straight to a full season order based on the pitch and materials alone
- One-hour: 50-60 pages. Half-hour: 22-35 pages
- Binge-watching expectation baked into structure — endings should pull hard to the next episode
- International appeal matters — streamers need global reach

### The Key Strategic Point (2025)
The traditional pilot season model is nearly dead. Networks are greenlighting fewer pilots. Streamers are doing straight-to-series increasingly.

**What this means for spec writers:** Your original pilot spec is primarily a writing sample and calling card, not a pilot you expect to get produced directly. Most pilots don't sell on spec. They get you representation, get you into rooms, and get you hired to write other shows or to pitch your concept with backing.

The pilot spec proves you can build a world. It's not the world you'll necessarily build.

## The Series Bible

When pitching a pilot, you often need a series bible alongside the script. The bible is the document that shows you've thought past episode one.

**What a series bible contains:**

**Logline** — one sentence for the series (same rules as feature logline)

**Series Overview** — 2-3 paragraphs: the world, the protagonist, the series engine, why this story now

**Tone and World** — what does this show feel like? What shows does it exist alongside? What's the visual language?

**Characters** — full profiles for all regular and recurring characters (same depth as the character profile template)

**Season One Arc** — the overarching story for the first season, episode by episode or in sequences. Where do we start? Where do we end? What's the season finale?

**Future Seasons** — 1-2 paragraphs each on seasons 2 and 3. Shows the engine runs.

**Episode Springboards** — 5-10 brief episode ideas (2-3 sentences each) that demonstrate the series engine in action

**Length:** 15-30 pages for a drama bible. 10-15 for a comedy bible.

## Feature vs. Pilot: Which Should You Write?

**Write a feature spec if:**
- Your idea has a clear beginning, middle, and end that resolves in 90-120 minutes
- The protagonist's arc completes — they transform, succeed, or fail definitively
- The dramatic question doesn't regenerate after being answered
- You want to target the spec sale market
- You want to enter Nicholl, Austin, or other feature contests

**Write a pilot spec if:**
- Your world generates infinite conflict by its nature
- Your protagonist has a wound that won't heal in 90 minutes — it's the source of ongoing drama
- The central dramatic question is structural (will the company survive? will the family stay together? can this person become who they need to be?) rather than episodic
- You want to get into TV rooms and fellowship programs
- You want to pursue the network and streaming fellowship programs (Disney/ABC, CBS, WB, NBC)

**The portfolio play (2025):** Have both. A strong feature spec and a strong original pilot together make you look like a writer with range. Fellowship programs want to see both.

## The Pilot Contests (Additional to the Feature Contests)

**Austin Film Festival TV Competition** — one-hour drama and half-hour comedy pilots. Separate from the feature competition. Strong industry presence.

**ScreenCraft Pilot Competition** — drama and comedy categories. Genre-specific options.

**Final Draft Big Break** — includes TV drama pilot category

**Sundance Episodic Lab** — for original limited series. Very competitive, very prestigious. Project needs to be in earlier stages of development.

**The WB TV Writing Workshop** — see CONTESTS-AND-FELLOWSHIPS.md — this is a fellowship program, not a contest, but it's the primary entry point for TV writing careers.

## The Pilot Spec vs. The "Spec Episode"

Two different things:

**Original pilot spec:** An entirely original show you created. Used to demonstrate your voice and your ability to build a world.

**Spec episode:** An episode of an existing show, written in that show's voice. Used historically to demonstrate you can write within someone else's world. Still used for some staff writer applications.

The industry has largely moved away from spec episodes as calling cards — showrunners know their own shows better than any spec can demonstrate, and a spec of their show is often more likely to irritate than impress them if it doesn't perfectly match the voice. Original pilots are now the primary writing sample.

The exception: if you're applying to a specific show's writing staff and they ask for a spec of a similar show (never a spec of their own show), the spec episode is still relevant.
