# What's Selling: 2025-2026

The market as of early 2025. Written from the perspective of someone tracking spec sales, reading the trades daily, and watching coverage come across a development desk.

---

## The Big Picture

**Update (2025):** The spec market is experiencing its biggest resurgence since 2019. Multiple scripts have gone to bidding wars, TV pilot development has slowed (too much supply, not enough greenlight), and studios have pivoted back toward acquiring strong original feature specs — including purchasing them without talent attached. The trend away from "packaging" (attaching stars before selling) is real: studios now prefer buying a clean spec and packaging it themselves. If you have the right script, the window is genuinely open right now.

The industry is still climbing out of the 2023 strike valley. Budgets are tighter, slates are smaller, and buyers are more selective than they were in the spec boom of 2019-2021. That's bad news if you're writing a $150M commercial spectacle on spec. It's potentially good news if you're writing something small, smart, and genuinely original.

The studios are still relying heavily on IP — sequels, franchises, adaptations. But the IP well is running dry on the big stuff. That's creating an opening for original spec material, particularly in genres that have proven theatrical legs without requiring $200M budgets.

Streaming is still the dominant force in development activity, but the days of "Netflix will buy anything" are over. Every major streamer has tightened their content spend significantly. They're looking for shows and films with clear audience signals — not just prestige projects.

## What's Hot Right Now

### Elevated Horror / Psychological Thriller
This is the most consistently active genre for spec sales. The A24/Blumhouse model has proven that smart horror can make enormous profits on modest budgets. A $15M horror film that opens to $20M is vastly more profitable than a $150M action film that opens to $50M.

What "elevated" means in 2025: genre mechanics (scares, tension, dread) combined with genuine thematic weight. Not horror that apologizes for itself, but horror that uses its mechanics to explore something real — grief, trauma, racism, identity, class anxiety.

**What's working:**
- Social horror (a social dynamic that becomes horrifying)
- Contained horror (limited locations, small cast)
- Inheritance horror (legacy, family trauma, what's passed down)
- Body horror with psychological underpinning

**Budget sweet spot:** $5M-$25M

**Recent comp space:** *Hereditary*, *Midsommar*, *Talk to Me*, *Nanny*, *Skinamarink*, *The Substance*

### Contained Thrillers
Two or three characters. One location (or limited locations). A ticking clock. A power dynamic that inverts.

This format works because it's cheap to produce, easy to cast, and doesn't require a VFX budget. A strong contained thriller written on spec can be produced for under $3M and still play in theaters or on streaming.

The audience for contained thrillers is loyal and predictable. When you're the buyer and your slate needs a film that will work, a tight contained thriller is reliable.

**What buyers want:** A killer premise that can be explained in one sentence. "Two people trapped in a car on a snowy mountain" is a premise. "A woman discovers the house she just bought contains evidence of her husband's secret" is a premise.

### Prestige Drama (Smart, Awards-Leaning)
Not going anywhere. The Oscars still drive enormous cultural conversation, and streamers need prestige content to win Emmy and Oscar campaigns that justify their subscriber counts.

But "prestige drama" in 2025 means something specific: it needs a clear hook, a star-attracting role, and a story that feels timely without being preachy. The days of slow-burn prestige dramas about middle-aged white men discovering themselves are genuinely over.

**What's working:**
- True crime with a fresh angle (not another podcast adaptation)
- Historical dramas with modern resonance
- Character studies with a high-concept twist
- Sports dramas (especially underdog stories with social stakes)

### Action-Comedy
The buddy movie is back. Not the sanitized PG-13 version — audiences want something with an edge. The success of *Nobody*, *The Fall Guy*, *Bullet Train* proved there's an audience for hard-R action-comedy that doesn't take itself too seriously.

Spec writers with a strong comedic voice and the ability to write set-pieces should be targeting this space. It's not easy, but a genuinely funny action spec is rare and will get read.

### Family Genre (Not Animation)
Live-action family films — PG-13 adventures, coming-of-age stories with genre elements — have been underserved by streaming and are starting to attract studio interest again. Disney's theatrical business, Universal's family releases, and the success of films like *Wonka* (before the discourse) suggest there's appetite.

### Female-Driven Genre
This has been a consistent trend for several years and is continuing. Female protagonists in genre films are no longer a marketing experiment — they're a market reality. Horror, action, thriller, sci-fi — buyers are actively seeking female-led genre material.

The mistake writers make: writing a male protagonist and gender-swapping them. A female protagonist needs to face obstacles that are specific to her experience. The best female-driven genre scripts understand that the protagonist's gender is integral to the story, not incidental.

## What's Dead or Cold

### The Big Original IP Play
Nobody is buying an original $150M franchise-starter from an unknown writer. The days of spec scripts selling in the $1M+ range to become tent-pole franchises are essentially over. The studios build those in-house.

This doesn't mean you shouldn't write big. It means if you're writing a huge sci-fi epic, think about whether it could be produced for under $20M, or whether there's a version of it that works as a limited series.

### Broad Romantic Comedy
The theatrical romcom is functionally dead. Streaming has been trying to revive it for years and mostly failing. The genre works on streaming as background noise, not as appointment television or theatrical event.

There are exceptions. A romcom with a truly fresh concept (not "two people meet cute and have misunderstandings") can still sell. But the standard romcom premise — attractive people fall in love, have a misunderstanding, resolve it — is a tough sell.

### Inspirational Sports Drama (Generic)
*Hoosiers*, *Remember the Titans*, the classic underdog sports film — these still work, but the bar is high and the market is saturated. If your sports drama is a true story, has a genuinely fresh angle, or operates as social commentary on top of the sports narrative, it can work. Generic inspiring sports drama? Pass.

### Whodunit Without a Twist
The classic Agatha Christie structure works when there's a genuine formal innovation or a fresh social milieu. *Knives Out* proved that. But a straight whodunit without a fresh angle is a very tough sell in 2025.

## The A24 / Blumhouse / Neon Model

These companies have defined the independent theatrical space and every development exec at every company is watching what they do.

**A24:** Prestige indie, elevated genre, distinctive directorial voice. They're looking for auteur-driven projects where the script is also the visual vision. The director and script are inseparable in their development model. Getting A24's attention as an unknown writer is extremely hard — they mostly work with directors who bring material. But their success has influenced what every indie-adjacent company is looking for.

**Blumhouse:** Low-budget horror and thriller. They have a hard budget cap ($6M and under for most projects). They're looking for contained, high-concept horror. Their model is volume — produce 8-10 films a year, a few break out. As a spec writer, a tight Blumhouse-compatible horror script is one of the more realistic paths to your first produced credit.

**Neon:** Genre-adjacent prestige. The distributors for *Parasite*, *Pig*, *The Substance*. They're not a development studio — they acquire completed or near-complete films. But understanding their aesthetic tells you what the independent theatrical market values.

## Streaming: Netflix, Amazon, Apple, Max

### Netflix
Still the most active buyer in the world, but the development philosophy has shifted. They want clear global appeal — stories with hooks that translate across cultures. They're specifically interested in:
- Action-thriller (the *Extraction* / *The Old Guard* model)
- Limited series adaptations of existing IP
- True crime documentary-adjacent drama
- Comedy with global reach

The unlimited-budget era is over. Every project has a harder internal ROI calculation. They're not buying prestige spec scripts to compete at the Oscars the way they were in 2018-2020.

### Amazon (MGM)
More conservative since the MGM acquisition. They're leaning into the MGM library and genre content. Action, thriller, mid-budget drama. The *Reacher* success story (big heroic genre character) is influencing their feature development.

### Apple TV+
The most prestige-focused streamer. Small slate, high quality. They're making films with major directors and stars — Scorsese, Fincher, et al. Very hard to break in as an unknown writer on the feature side. Their TV development is slightly more accessible.

### Max (Warner Bros. Discovery)
Shifting focus toward theatrical and premium content after years of chaos. The WB theatrical machine is back to prioritizing the DC slate and franchise films. Independent/mid-budget spec material is harder to get traction at Warner Bros. than it was five years ago.

## Micro-Budget Path

If you're an unknown writer who also wants to direct, the micro-budget path is real.

A micro-budget feature (under $500K) that you can make with local crew, limited locations, and no stars can:
- Get into festivals (Sundance, SXSW, Tribeca)
- Get you an agent and manager
- Get you a meeting at every company in town
- Lead directly to your next, better-funded film

The key: a $300K film needs to look like a $3M film. That means the contained format, a strong visual approach, and a script that can be fully realized within the budget.

Films to study: *Monsters* (Gareth Edwards), *Tangerine* (Sean Baker), *Good Time* (Safdie Brothers), *I Think We're Alone Now*, *The Witch* (early Robert Eggers).

The micro-budget path is not "compromise to what you can afford." It's "find a story that can only be told small." The best micro-budget films are better for their constraints.

## What This Means for Your Spec

Write something that is:

1. **Contained** — 2-3 main characters, 1-3 locations, achievable on a real budget
2. **Genre** — it fits a clear, marketable category (not "a character study")
3. **Original** — not an adaptation of something that already exists, unless you own it
4. **Timely but not preachy** — it speaks to something real in the culture without lecturing
5. **Castable** — it has a role that a working actor would want to play
6. **Loglinable** — it can be described in one compelling sentence

If you can check all six boxes, you have a commercial spec. Whether it sells or not is then a question of execution.

## 2025 Market Signals (From the Trades)

- **Spec sales are up** — significant resurgence after the 2023 WGA/SAG strikes suppressed the market for 18 months
- **Features over TV pilots** — the TV development market is oversaturated; features are the smarter spec play right now
- **Genre blending is expected** — "psychological thriller with horror elements" or "sci-fi drama with mystery mechanics" is standard. Pure genre plays are less interesting than genre combinations
- **Production-ready = competitive edge** — polished, final-draft-quality scripts that feel shootable get read faster than "promising but rough"
- **The bidding war is back** — high-concept thrillers have triggered multi-studio auctions in 2024-2025, the first significant bidding wars since pre-strike
- **Diversity of voice is a genuine market signal** — not just optics. Stories by and about underrepresented communities are actively sought, particularly by streamers who need global reach
- **The "straight spec sale" is preferred** — studios would rather buy a clean spec and attach talent themselves than inherit a package they didn't choose
